1. Why this tab matters

Most financial models for subscription businesses focus on revenue. This tab focuses on the liability side: money owed to users who paid but haven't yet consumed what they bought.

Carryover creates three distinct problems that must be managed simultaneously:

ProblemWhat it meansHow this tab addresses it
Balance sheet liabilityEvery GB of carried-over data is a future supplier cost we must pay when the user redeems it. At 5K users this is ~$50K sitting on the books.Per-tier liability table; concentration risk analysis
Perception / churn riskUsers who see "6 GB unused" every month feel they are wasting money and cancel — even when they are mathematically getting excellent value.Churn spiral model; UX fix (Option 3)
Revenue recognitionCash received for subscriptions is NOT revenue until the service is delivered. Carryover creates deferred revenue on the books that requires proper accounting treatment.Four journal entries; monthly close checklist

Discussion framing — 2026-06-02

The session opened with the core question: is carryover data a liability or a feature? The answer is both. It is a feature that sells subscriptions (users love the safety net) and a liability that requires disciplined management. The session worked through both dimensions in detail, and the key insight was that the same-SIM dynamic allocation architecture we are already building for procurement reasons (Tab 15) completely changes the carryover options — including unlocking UAE home redemption.

3. Aiko's $4.03 breakdown — full worked example

Aiko — Premium Economy subscriber, Emirates cabin crew

Aiko flies mostly short-haul routes. She uses about 4 GB per month from her 10 GB pool. The remaining 6 GB sit unused at the end of the billing cycle. Here is what happens next.

  1. Pool size
    Aiko's Premium Economy plan gives her a 10 GB monthly pool. She pays $17.99/month. She has already paid — this is a done deal.
  2. Consumption this month
    She uses 40% of her pool. 10 GB x 40% = 4 GB consumed. We paid the wholesale supplier for these 4 GB when she used them (dynamic allocation model, see section 10).
  3. Unused balance
    10 GB pool minus 4 GB used = 6 GB unused. Under 0% carryover, these expire and we keep the margin (about $8.06 pure margin from unused capacity). Under Premium's 50% carry rule, exactly half carries forward.
  4. Carry calculation
    50% carry applied to 6 GB unused = 3 GB carried forward. The other 3 GB expire — that is our expired margin (see section 7).
  5. Our liability
    We owe Aiko 3 GB of future data that we have not yet paid our supplier for. When she uses those 3 GB, we trigger a top-up call to the supplier API and pay them.

    3 GB x $1.3425/GB = $4.0275 ≈ $4.03

    This $4.03 is NOT a loss. It is a deferred cost — a service we collected revenue for but have not yet paid the supplier for. It sits as a liability until redeemed or expired.

The $4.03 is our cost, not a selling price — this tripped us up early

There was initial confusion about whether $4.03 is what we owe the supplier or what we charge the user. It is our cost from the wholesale supplier. Aiko already paid us $17.99 in full. The $4.03 is the future out-of-pocket we will incur when she redeems those 3 GB. We do not charge her again — she already paid for this. The liability is purely on the cost side of our books.

6. Balraj's gifting idea

Balraj's gifting idea — three wins in one mechanic

Let users gift their carryover pool (not their main monthly pool) to family or colleagues. This is not a standard eSIM feature — it is a differentiator that simultaneously solves the liability problem, the churn problem, and the acquisition problem.

7. Risk 1 reframe — expired margin is real money

Balraj's second major insight: data that does NOT carry over is pure margin we keep. The user paid for capacity they did not use. We never paid the supplier for it. This is not a risk — it is a feature of the pricing model that is working exactly as designed.

Balraj's reframe (direct quote captured)

"So the 50% that doesn't carry over — that's not our liability. That's margin we already earned and will never have to pay out. The carryover is the liability. The expiry is the profit. We want more expiry, not less."

This reframe is correct and important. Premium and Business are designed with partial carryover precisely to generate expired margin. First/Suite/Cockpit at 100% carry deliberately sacrifice this expired margin in exchange for positioning as "fully generous" premium tiers.

8. Balraj's "100% carry + short expiry" strategy

Balraj's third major insight: "100% carryover" is a powerful marketing headline. Nobody refuses 100% carryover. But the fine print — "must use within X days" — determines how much expired margin we actually capture. You can say 100% carry and still generate significant expired margin if the redemption window is short enough.

The marketing trick explained

Marketing headline: "100% carryover on all Premium tiers — every GB you don't use carries forward."
Fine print: "Carried-over GB must be used within 30 days of carryover."

Users focus on the "100%" and perceive the product as generous. The 30-day window is reasonable and defensible — it is not a gotcha. But it does generate significant expired margin from users who hoard data but then do not fly within the window.

9. Corrected analysis — 2-month cap is fine (recommendation reversed)

This section documents a correction made during the session. The initial recommendation of "add a 30-day expiry to capture $17.45/user expired margin" was wrong. Here is the corrected math.

Balraj's correction (this is the key insight of the session)

Balraj pushed back: "But with a 2-month cap, the data does eventually expire anyway. What is the actual steady-state difference between a 30-day window and a 2-month cap?"

The answer: in steady state, the expired margin is the same. With a 2-month cap, a user who does not fly for 2 months loses all their carryover. The only difference is timing — when the margin appears on the books — and peak balance sheet liability.

The real comparison: 30-day vs 2-month policy

Peak balance sheet liability (per user)~$18 (30-day accumulation)~$72 (2 months accumulating)2-month cap has 4x higher peak liability
Marketing headline"Carry up to 30 days""2 full months of carryover"2-month is dramatically better marketing
User perception"Tight window, feels stingy""Genuinely generous policy"2-month wins on perception
RecommendationRejectedKeep 2-month capRight answer is the current policy
Final position (locked): Keep 2-month carryover cap for First and First Suite. Do not add a short expiry window. The marketing wins ("2 full months of carryover") outweigh the liability timing concern. In steady state, expired margin from the 2-month cap is actually higher than the 30-day alternative. Balraj's intuition was correct; the initial recommendation was wrong.
The balance sheet peak of $72 vs $18 sounds alarming until you realize it resolves itself naturally within 2 months as users fly. The key operational requirement is to hold enough liquid cash to cover the peak liability at all times — approximately $50K at 5,000 users.

10. Risk 2 — same-SIM dynamic allocation (technical architecture)

Industry standard architecture — Airalo, Holafly, Crew SIM, eSIM Access all do this

When a user has a 10 GB pool and uses 1 GB, does that 1 GB come from the same eSIM profile or a new one? Answer: same eSIM, dynamically topped up via supplier API. This is how every major eSIM platform works.

How the flow works

  1. User signs up. We provision ONE eSIM profile. Example ICCID: 8901260... We pre-load minimal data (or 0 GB) on it.
  2. App UI shows the user their full pool balance — for example, 10 GB for Premium Economy. This is a balance in our database, not pre-loaded on the physical eSIM.
  3. User activates data for a trip. Backend calls supplier API: "add 1 GB to ICCID 8901260..." The eSIM gains 1 GB of active capacity.
  4. User uses the data. Supplier bills us for the top-up trigger (matches Option A pay-per-order model from Tab 15).
  5. App decrements the pool balance in our database. User sees the pool shrink as they use it.

Why this matters for carryover liability

Key benefitDetail
We only pay when triggered10,000 signups = $0 supplier cost. We pay only when users actually pull data from the pool. Carryover balance sits in our database, not on the eSIM. No pre-payment to supplier for unredemed carryover.
Pool feels like a savings accountUX psychology: users see a balance they can draw from whenever they fly. This is a retention mechanic. They feel they have something to lose if they cancel.
Supplier minimum block sizeMost suppliers set minimums: 1 GB, 100 MB, or 50 MB per top-up call. If minimum is 1 GB, a user who only uses 300 MB still triggers a 1 GB charge. Need to confirm with chosen supplier before building.

The dynamic allocation architecture is the enabling technology for both the gifting feature (section 6) and the UAE redemption feature (section 11). Without same-SIM dynamic provisioning, both would require a second physical eSIM — much more complex. With it, they are just routing decisions at the database layer.

11. Risk 3 — UAE redemption via dynamic allocation (Balraj's key insight)

Balraj's UAE insight — carryover is not tied to geography

Because carryover balance is stored in our database (not on the physical eSIM), it has no inherent geographic restriction. We can route a redemption to any country-specific eSIM we provision. This means crew can redeem carryover data in Dubai — where they spend 70% of their time — not just on their next international flight.

13. Accounting + bookkeeping deep-dive (self-funded owner guide)

This section is written for a self-funded operator, not an investor deck

Balraj confirmed during the session: Peanut is self-funded. No fundraise. The accounting framework here is designed for an owner-operator who wants to run clean books without over-engineering Year 1. The goal is: accurate enough to make decisions, simple enough to actually do it.

The four journal entries for a single subscription cycle (Aiko's January)

When Aiko pays for Premium Economy in January, four accounting events happen across the month.

Entry 1 — January 1: Cash received
DR Cash ............... $17.99
CR Deferred Revenue .. $17.99

We have Aiko's money but have not yet delivered the service. It sits as a liability (Deferred Revenue) until delivered.
Entry 2 — January (as data is consumed): Revenue recognition
DR Deferred Revenue ... $17.99
CR Revenue ........... $17.99

As Aiko uses her 10 GB pool throughout January, we recognize revenue. Simplified: recognize the full $17.99 at month-end since the billing cycle is monthly. More precise: prorate daily.
Entry 3 — January (as data is consumed): COGS
DR COGS (Supplier Cost) ... $5.37
CR Accounts Payable (Supplier) ... $5.37

4 GB used x $1.3425/GB = $5.37. We record this when Aiko triggers each top-up call. The supplier bills us at month-end or per-trigger depending on contract terms.
Entry 4 — February 1: Carryover liability recognized
DR Carryover Data Reserve ... $4.03
CR Service Liability (Carryover) ... $4.03

3 GB carried forward x $1.3425/GB = $4.03. We record the future obligation to deliver this data. When Aiko redeems it, we debit Service Liability and credit Accounts Payable (Supplier).

Year 1 simplification: track carryover in a spreadsheet

The big simplification for <1,000 users

With under 1,000 users, the total carryover liability is unlikely to exceed $20,000. At this scale, a Google Sheet with the following columns is all you need:

  • User ID | Tier | GB Carried | $ Liability | Expiry Date | Status (active / redeemed / expired)

Update this sheet at the end of each billing cycle. The total of the $ Liability column is what you report on the balance sheet as "Service Liability — Data Carryover." No fancy accounting software required until you hit $50K annual revenue.

14. Strategic takeaways

Takeaway 1 — Premium and Business carry design is correct

50% and 70% partial carryover generates $4.03 and $3.32 per user per month in expired margin. This is intentional and valuable. Do not change these parameters. The partial carry is the mechanism by which these tiers generate above-average margin per subscriber relative to full-carry tiers.

Takeaway 2 — First Suite concentration risk is real but the 2-month cap is fine

First Suite at $35.99/user is the biggest per-user liability in the model. At 3% penetration of 100K users, that is $108K from one tier. However, the 2-month cap means the liability resolves itself within two billing cycles. The key operational requirement is holding liquid cash equal to the total carryover balance at all times. The math long-term is the same as a 30-day window; only peak timing differs.

Takeaway 3 — Same-SIM dynamic allocation is the enabling architecture

Build the eSIM provisioning layer on dynamic allocation from day one. This is industry standard (Airalo, Holafly, Crew SIM all do it). It means we only pay the supplier when users actually consume data. The carryover "balance" is a number in our database. This unlocks gifting, UAE redemption, and any future redemption channel without re-architecting the eSIM layer.

Takeaway 4 — Three carryover channels, each solving a different strategic problem

Next-trip redemption preserves natural usage. UAE home redemption drives daily engagement and eliminates churn perception. Gifting creates viral acquisition and turns a liability into a growth channel. Build all three. Next-trip is v1.0 (already implied). UAE and gifting are v1.1/v1.2 depending on supplier validation.

Takeaway 5 — Year 1 bookkeeping: Wave + a Google Sheet is sufficient

Under 1,000 users, total carryover liability is under $20K. Track it in a spreadsheet. Use Wave (free) for cash-basis accounting. Run the 7-step monthly close. Upgrade to QuickBooks at $50K annual revenue. Bring in a CPA for the annual Texas LLC filing and when carryover liability exceeds $100K. Do not over-engineer the accounting before you have users.

Note on the 20% redemption rate placeholder

The model currently uses a 20% monthly redemption rate for carryover data. This means 80% of carried-over data is never redeemed — it expires after the cap period. There is significant uncertainty here:
