1. Why this tab matters — and the timing split

Tab 21 exists to answer three "what if" questions that the base P&L model ignores by design. The base model assumes steady-state: fixed wholesale markup, fixed crew base, fixed prices. Tab 21 tests what happens when those assumptions change.

The three sections map to three different strategic timelines:

SectionQuestionTimingPriority
1 — Wholesale Triggers When does scale unlock better supplier rates? Year 2+ (need 5K users first) PARKED
2 — Multi-Airline What does expansion to Etihad, JAL, ANA look like? Year 2-3 planning PARKED
3 — Competitive Floor How LOW can we go before we lose money per tier? NOW — affects launch pricing URGENT
Why Section 3 matters right now: We are setting launch prices today. If Crew SIM announces a promotional rate after launch, we need to know instantly — without recalculating — whether we can follow them, and on which tiers. Section 3 gives us that answer pre-computed for every tier.
Why Sections 1 and 2 are parked: Section 1 requires real user data to negotiate (no leverage at 0 users). Section 2 requires Section 1 to land first — you expand airlines when your first airline is proven. Neither is actionable today.

5. Section 3 — Competitive Floor NOW — Critical

The question this section answers: "If Crew SIM undercuts our retail price on any tier today, exactly how low can we follow before that tier loses money?"

The floor formula

Min Viable Price = COGS + OPEX per user per month ($1.35)
At this price: net profit = $0. We break even and nothing more.

This is not a target — it is a hard floor. Pricing below this number means we lose money on every transaction at that tier.

The chart: cushion by tier

What the numbers tell us

Most tiers have significant room to maneuver. The problem tier is Economy:

  • Economy: $0.28 cushion. Crew SIM at $4.99, our floor at $4.71. We are 28 cents from the edge on our entry tier. This is the only tier where a Crew SIM price cut could immediately threaten our business model.
  • All subscription tiers: Premium ($6.27), Business ($11.58), First ($25.22), Cockpit ($13.97) — all have room to absorb promotional moves without going negative.
  • Roster Bundle: $26.94 cushion. Crew SIM charges $35 for their equivalent. We already win on price by $15. No defensive action needed.

6. The 8-feature moat

Before discussing any defensive pricing move, we should note that Crew SIM's competitive threat is limited by what they can offer. Our moat is not price — it is the feature stack they cannot replicate without rebuilding their entire product.

  • 1. CrewLink Roster UploadHigh switching cost. Ties Peanut to crew workflow. Once a crew member has uploaded their roster, Peanut becomes part of their pre-flight routine. No competitor builds for this.
  • 2. Gifting CarryoverViral mechanic. Unused data becomes a gift to another crew member. Creates a loop: user stays subscribed because giving is frictionless, recipient signs up.
  • 3. UAE RedemptionUse eSIM data at home in Dubai via dynamic allocation. Turns a travel-only product into daily-life infrastructure. No standard eSIM provider enables this.
  • 4. Crew Recs (Michelin-style)Content moat. Crew-curated destination restaurant, hotel, and experience database. Only Peanut users can contribute and access. Content quality compounds over time.
  • 5. Same-SIM Dynamic AllocationOne eSIM, multiple country pools, allocated dynamically. Better UX than the industry standard of one eSIM per country. This is the architectural enabler for UAE redemption and gifting.
  • 6. 60% Cheaper Than 1 GB PacketsSubscription pools beat per-GB pricing for anyone flying more than twice a month. The cost advantage compounds over a year — crew figure this out quickly.
  • 7. AI SupportInstant eSIM installation help, top-up, refunds via in-app AI chat. Crew SIM is email-only. For crew on a 48-hour layover who can't get their SIM working, speed of support is everything.
  • 8. Annual Cockpit PrepayOne payment, one year, locked retention. The user is off the competitive market entirely. No monthly renewal friction. No comparison-shopping window for a competitor to exploit.

8. The 4-step playbook if attacked

This playbook is sequential. Do not skip to Step 3 without completing Steps 1 and 2. Most competitive moves resolve at Step 2 without any action on our part.

  • Days 1-7
    Don't react publicly. Watch the move.

    Do not post, announce, or even mention a competitive response. Gather data on exactly what Crew SIM changed. A knee-jerk response signals weakness and may over-react to a test or typo.

  • Week 2-4
    Assess actual impact. Track signups and churn.

    If neither new signups nor churn moves in Week 3, ignore completely. The vast majority of competitive price changes have zero measurable impact on crew because crew choose based on trust and features, not a 50-cent price difference.

  • Only if signups drop
    "Spring Promotion" — looks organic, not reactive.

    Options: "1 month free Premium for new crew signing up in [month]" or "Refer 3 friends, get Cockpit at 50% for Year 1." Never cut posted prices. The promotion window ends and the full price remains intact.

  • Parallel to Step 3
    Aggressive retention for at-risk users.

    Target individuals, not the whole base. Premium users 6+ months: push to Cockpit annual (locks them, removes them from the risk pool entirely). Heavy users: offer free CrewLink upgrade. Do not blanket discount — that trains your best users to wait for sales.

9. Economy as the exposed tier

Economy deserves a dedicated section because it is structurally different from every other tier:

Why Economy is uniquely exposed

  • PAYG structure — no monthly subscription lock-in
  • Price-sensitive users actively comparison-shop between purchases
  • $0.28 cushion — nearly no room to maneuver
  • Cannot run "Spring Promotion" effectively (no ongoing relationship to leverage)
  • First impression tier — losing Economy users means losing the pipeline to subscriptions

The hard rules for Economy

  • Hard floor: $4.71 (COGS $3.36 + OPEX $1.35)
  • Never price below $4.71 under any circumstances — we lose money on every transaction
  • If Crew SIM goes below $4.99, match at $4.99 or slightly below
  • If Crew SIM goes below $4.71, flag for pricing architecture review — we cannot follow
  • Monitor Crew SIM Economy pricing monthly

10. The big insight — bundle not price

The strategic principle
We compete on the bundle, not on price.

Crew SIM has 41 countries on a price list. We have 41 countries plus CrewLink roster sync, gifting carryover, UAE redemption, Crew Recs, AI support, same-SIM dynamic allocation, and Annual Cockpit lock-in. That 8-feature delta is not something Crew SIM can close with a price cut — they would have to rebuild their entire product.

The implication for pricing defense: the more we lean into the bundle rather than price matching, the stronger our competitive position becomes. A crew member who has uploaded their roster, saved $40 vs PAYG, gifted unused data to a colleague, and used UAE redemption once is not going to leave for a competitor offering a $1 discount. They are locked in by value, not by price.

Price matching is only a tool of last resort for Economy — the one tier where we have no subscription relationship and the user has no sunk cost in our product.
