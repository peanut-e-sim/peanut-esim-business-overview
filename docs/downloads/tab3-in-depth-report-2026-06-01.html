Tab 3 in one sentence

Tab 3 translates the retail prices from Tab 1 and the consumption behavior from Tab 2 into a single per-USER profit number for each tier. It is the bridge between architecture and money. Every downstream scaling tab (Volume Scenarios, 24-Month Rollout, LTV-CAC) multiplies these per-user numbers by user counts.

This document captures the full structure of Tab 3 — Per-Tier Unit Economics of the Peanut eSIM P&L Model. Tab 3 answers a single question: "For one subscriber on each plan, in one month, how much profit do we make?" The walkthrough explains all 13 columns (3 column groups), the locked per-tier results, the strategic insights about which tier wins, and the downstream tabs that depend on these numbers.

Why OPEX is $1.80

The $1.80 figure comes from Tab 1 Section 5 (Operating Cost per User per Month). It bundles payment processing ($1.20 mixed Stripe/Apple IAP), customer support amortized ($0.30), cloud/API ($0.15), SMS/OTP ($0.05), hosting ($0.10). This is the same number applied across every tier — the assumption being that ops cost scales linearly per active user, regardless of plan size.

4. Strategic insights

4.1 First Suite is the hero

At $33.00 gross profit / user / month, First Suite generates more absolute profit than any other tier — beating even Cockpit's annual prepay on a monthly-equivalent basis. The reason is structural: First Suite has the same friction-capped consumption as First (~13 GB) but a $10 higher retail price. The pool went from 25 GB to 40 GB on paper, but actual GB delivered stayed flat. Pool expansion is free; price expansion is profit.

4.2 Business is the default RECOMMENDED tier

Business sits at the sweet spot: 70% gross margin, $21.84 profit per user, the highest absolute profit among the broad-market tiers. It's the "everyone should buy this" tier on the price card — high enough that ARPU is meaningful, low enough that broad crew demographics can afford it. This is why it carries the RECOMMENDED flag in the app.

4.3 First is the weakest tier on margin

First's net margin is just 52.3% — the lowest of any subscription tier. The reason: heavy users on First push consumption to 13.5 GB (54% of the 25 GB pool), and at $1.34/GB blended that's $18+ of COGS against just $39.99 revenue. First's job is positional — it bridges Business to First Suite on the price ladder. It captures users who feel above Business but balk at First Suite's $49.99 price tag.

First is the "ladder rung" tier. Its purpose is to make First Suite look reasonable. We accept thinner margins here because it pulls Business buyers up.

4.4 Economy is a volume play

At $3.65 gross profit per top-up, Economy needs 2.5+ purchases per month to compete with even the cheapest subscription tier. A user who buys 2 top-ups in a month delivers $7.30 — less than half of Premium Economy. The strategic role of Economy is twofold: (1) acquisition — a $4.99 try-it price has near-zero friction, and (2) upsell anchor — once users buy 3 top-ups in a month ($14.97), they realize Premium Economy ($17.99 for 10 GB) is a better deal and convert.

4.5 Cockpit is about cash flow, not per-user max profit

At $25.67 monthly-equivalent revenue and $15.80 gross profit, Cockpit underperforms Business ($21.84) and First Suite ($33.00) on a per-user-per-month basis. That's by design. Cockpit's value is $299 collected upfront on day 1 — strong cash flow, zero churn for 12 months, predictable annual revenue stream. The annual lock-in matters more than per-month margin maximization.

4.6 The upgrade arithmetic

Every upgrade path has a measurable per-user-per-month profit delta:

Upgrade pathProfit deltaAnnual delta per user
Economy → Premium Economy+$9.70/mo+$116/yr
Premium Economy → Business+$8.49/mo+$102/yr
Business → First+$1.49/mo+$18/yr (weak)
Business → First Suite+$11.18/mo+$134/yr
First → First Suite+$9.67/mo+$116/yr

Implication: the strongest in-app upgrade prompt to design is Business → First Suite, skipping First entirely. Marketing should funnel Business users directly to First Suite, not nudge them through First.
