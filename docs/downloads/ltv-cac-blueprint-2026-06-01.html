1. Why this tab matters

LTV:CAC is the investor's first question: "For every dollar you spend acquiring a user, how many dollars do you get back over their lifetime?" Most consumer subscription businesses consider a 3:1 ratio healthy and 5:1 excellent. Peanut's ratios are 32x to 760x.

But there is a critical nuance: great unit economics don't automatically mean a great business. They mean the business is efficient per unit. The total business value depends on how many units you can acquire. That scale question is the real test — and it hinges entirely on whether word-of-mouth within the Emirates crew network compounds fast enough.

Cockpit modeling correction — important to document
An earlier version of the model applied the monthly churn formula to Cockpit: 1 / (monthly churn %) = lifetime. But Cockpit has no monthly churn — it is annual prepay. Applying any monthly churn rate to Cockpit produces absurd results (e.g., at 0.6% monthly "churn" = 167-month lifetime, which is 14 years). This was a modeling error. 4. CAC structure and the fix (key discussion)

Original model (wrong): had paid social + influencer baked in

The original CAC calculation included an allocation for paid social advertising ($12 CAC per paid user) and influencer marketing ($8 CAC per influencer-acquired user). This produced a blended CAC of approximately $3.85.

Rejected: the $3.85 blended CAC with paid social + influencer
Reason: Balraj confirmed that the launch plan is word-of-mouth only. No paid ads at launch. The Harikai illustrator partnership is an asset swap (she provides illustrations, we give exposure/credits), not a paid CAC event. Including paid channels in launch CAC was incorrect and overstated the cost.

Corrected model (locked 2026-06-01):

Channel% of acquisition$ CACNotes
Organic / crew word-of-mouth85%$0Cabin crew layover chat, group chats, social proof. 100% free.
Referral bonus ($5 credit)15%$5Existing user refers a colleague. Paid in app credit, not cash.
Paid social0%—FUTURE channel. Not in launch plan. Will increase blended CAC when added.
Influencer / crew creator0%—FUTURE. Harikai partnership is asset swap, not paid CAC.
Blended CAC (launch)100%$0.7585% × $0 + 15% × $5 = $0.75

Why $5 referral credit is still a CAC even though it's not cash

The $5 referral credit appears in the app as a credit against the user's next bill — it is an economic cost, not a cash outflow in the month it's granted. But over time, as users redeem those credits, it reduces realized revenue. We treat it as CAC = $5 because it is the economic cost of the acquisition event, even though the cash impact is deferred and fractional.

6. The discussion we had

Discussion — why the same $0.75 CAC applies to all tiers

A crucial insight emerged during this walkthrough: acquisition and tier selection are sequential, not simultaneous. When Akito tells his crew friend about Peanut, the friend downloads the app before choosing a plan. The $0.75 CAC is incurred at the download/signup event, not at the tier selection event. Tier selection happens in the onboarding flow afterwards.

Implication: every tier upgrade is $0 additional CAC. An Economy user who upgrades to Premium after 2 months adds $83 to their LTV at zero incremental acquisition cost. The upgrade is pure profit from the business's perspective. This makes the conversion funnel (Economy to subscription) even more valuable than it first appears — it is not just a revenue line, it is a pure-margin event.

Discussion — the Cockpit modeling error and the fix

The original model applied a monthly churn assumption to Cockpit. Balraj noticed this during the walkthrough — a Cockpit user who prepays $299/year cannot churn in month 1. The monthly churn formula only applies to rolling subscription products. For Cockpit, we need to model the actual renewal pattern: Year 1 = 100% retention (prepaid), Year 2+ = ~70% renewal rate.

We considered: what is the realistic average lifetime for an annual-prepay subscriber with 70% renewal? The geometric series with 70% renewal converges to approximately 3 years (36 months) as the expected lifetime. This is the number now used. The old formula would have produced 167 months (14 years) which was clearly wrong.

Discussion — is the $0.75 CAC sustainable at scale?

Word-of-mouth has a natural ceiling. At some point, Akito's personal network is exhausted. The first degree of referrals also reaches saturation. From M12+ the blended CAC will rise as the business starts adding paid acquisition channels to accelerate growth beyond organic reach.

We modeled this as a future-state concern, not a launch concern. The $0.75 CAC is realistic for M1-M12 based on the crew network thesis. What we did not model: what happens to the LTV:CAC ratio when CAC rises to $15-25 (typical for paid social). Answer: it is still excellent. Even at $25 CAC, Premium LTV:CAC = 134/25 = 5.4x — which is above the "excellent" threshold. The business remains healthy even with paid acquisition.

However: this means the business model does NOT depend on always staying at $0.75. The unit economics work even at competitive CAC levels. This is a strength.

Discussion — why we separated CAC from tier CAC

We briefly considered whether to model different CAC levels for different tier users — e.g., a Business user might be harder to find and convert than an Economy trial user. Rejected this approach for two reasons: (1) in the word-of-mouth model, we don't control who Akito's friend tells (it could be a purser who immediately goes to Business or a junior crew who tries Economy), and (2) the math doesn't support it — there is no empirical evidence that higher-tier users cost more to acquire in a WOM model. If anything, senior crew (more likely to buy Business+) are more connected to other senior crew via professional networks, so higher-tier users may actually have lower CAC.

8. The lemonade stand payback analogy

From the source xlsx, verbatim:

You spend $5 on posters to attract a customer. Each lemonade they buy earns you $2 profit. After 3 lemonades ($6 cumulative), you have paid back the $5 poster cost. Every lemonade after that = pure profit.

Applied to Peanut, Premium tier:

  • Posters (CAC): $0.75
  • Each lemonade (monthly profit): $13.36
  • Payback: $0.75 / $13.36 = 0.056 months = approximately 1.7 days
  • Everything after day 2 of a user's active month = pure profit

Compare to a typical startup:

  • CAC: $50 (typical paid social)
  • Monthly profit: $5
  • Payback: 10 months
  • Many startups don't survive 10 months of negative unit economics at scale

Peanut's word-of-mouth strategy is not just cheap — it structurally eliminates the payback-period risk that kills most subscription startups.

10. The real risk: scale velocity

The key insight from this tab's discussion:

The risk is NOT unit economics — it is whether word-of-mouth alone can scale fast enough to reach 5,000 users in 24 months.

The math behind this risk:

Referral rateDescriptionUsers at M18Users at M24
0.5 referrals/user/moEach active user tells 1 new person every 2 months~5,000~5,000+ (target achieved)
0.3 referrals/user/moModerate crew word-of-mouth~3,200~4,800
0.2 referrals/user/moConservative crew word-of-mouth~2,000~2,400

The 5,000-user M24 target requires roughly 0.5 referrals per user per month in the steady-state growth period. That means, on average, each active user needs to tell one new person about Peanut every 2 months. In a tight crew community with monthly layovers and shared accommodations, this is plausible — but it is not guaranteed. The crew-thesis must hold.

12. Strategic takeaway

  1. The unit economics are a structural advantage — use them in investor conversations. A 179x LTV:CAC ratio on the main subscription tier is genuinely exceptional. Most consumer SaaS businesses would be excited with 10x. Peanut's structural advantage (low CAC via WOM + high margin via breakage) creates an unusually efficient business. This should be front and center in any pitch.
  2. Instrument the referral loop from day one. Since scale velocity is the main risk, Balraj needs to measure referral rate (new user / existing user) every month from M1. Track this as a core KPI, not a nice-to-have. If the referral rate is below 0.3 at M3, either the product isn't driving WOM (product quality issue) or we're not in the right community (acquisition quality issue). Either way, it needs diagnosis before M6.
  3. The upgrade funnel is worth more than it looks. Because CAC is the same ($0.75) regardless of tier, every Economy-to-subscription upgrade is pure LTV at zero additional cost. An Economy user who upgrades to Business after 2 months adds +$192 LTV for free. This makes the in-app conversion nudge (show Premium value after 3 PAYG purchases) one of the highest-ROI product features in the entire roadmap.
