1Three concepts you need to know

LTV, CAC, and Churn — the three numbers that tell you whether a subscription business is healthy.

Churn
% of users who quit per month
If 100 users start January and 18 quit by February, monthly churn = 18%. Lower churn = users stay longer = more revenue per acquisition.
Lifetime Value (LTV)
Gross profit × Avg months active
If a Premium user earns us $13.36/month gross profit and stays 10 months on average, LTV = $133.60. This is the total profit we earn from one user.
Acquisition Cost (CAC)
$ spent to acquire one user
Peanut's blended CAC at launch: $0.75. 85% of users arrive organically (crew word-of-mouth, $0), 15% via referral bonus ($5 credit). Weighted average = $0.75.
The Magic Ratio
LTV / CAC
Greater than 3 = healthy. Greater than 5 = excellent. Greater than 10 = exceptional. Peanut's ratios are 68x to 760x. This business is exceptionally efficient at acquiring and keeping users.

2Monthly churn by tier

Higher tiers churn less. Users who invest in a bigger plan are more committed. The exception: Cockpit is annual prepay — it doesn't use monthly churn math at all.

Monthly churn rate by tier

Cockpit is excluded — it's annual prepay. Churn analysis doesn't apply the same way.

Cockpit — special case (annual prepay) Cockpit is $299/year, prepaid upfront. Year 1 is forced 0% churn (they already paid). After Year 1, roughly 70% renew. Realistic average lifetime: 36 months (3 years). We modeled LTV as $15.82/month contribution × 36 months = $569.52. We do NOT apply the monthly churn formula to Cockpit — the formula would produce an absurd 167-month lifetime, which was a modeling error caught and corrected in this version.

4Payback period — the lemonade stand analogy

"How long does a new user need to stay before they earn back what it cost to acquire them?"

The lemonade stand

You spend $5 on posters to attract a customer. Each lemonade earns you $2 profit. After 3 lemonades ($6 cumulative), you have paid back the $5 poster cost. Every lemonade after that = pure profit.

5CAC breakdown — how we acquire users

Launch is 100% word-of-mouth. No paid ads. No influencer fees. Just Akito telling crew, and crew telling crew.

Acquisition channel mix at launch

85% organic (free), 15% referral ($5 credit). Paid social and influencer channels are $0 at launch — future only.

Economy churn pressure-test

Even at 40% monthly churn (extreme pessimist), LTV:CAC stays excellent because $0.75 CAC is so low.

Why CAC is $0.75 for all tiers: Acquisition happens before tier selection. When Akito tells his colleague Yuki about Peanut, Yuki downloads the app before choosing a plan. The $0.75 CAC applies to Yuki regardless of whether she picks Economy or Business. This means every tier upgrade is pure incremental LTV at zero additional acquisition cost. Economy to Premium adds $83 LTV at $0 extra CAC.

6The real risk — scale velocity, not unit economics

The unit economics are excellent at every tier. The risk is whether word-of-mouth can compound fast enough.

Referral velocity matters — two growth paths

If each user refers 0.5 new users/month: 5,000 users by M18. If each refers only 0.2/month: only ~2,400 by M24.

The honest verdict
The math is not the risk.
Every tier produces an LTV:CAC ratio that most consumer businesses would be ecstatic to achieve. The risk is whether word-of-mouth within Emirates crew circles compounds fast enough. That is a social network problem, not an economics problem.
