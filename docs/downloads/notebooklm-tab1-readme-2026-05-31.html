TAB 1 — README & ASSUMPTIONS Peanut eSIM P&L Model Source Document for NotebookLM Prepared: May 31, 2026 — Research-backed revision INTRODUCTION This document explains the first tab of the Peanut eSIM financial model — the README and Assumptions tab. Think of Tab 1 as the control panel for the entire spreadsheet. Every revenue projection, every cost calculation, every scenario comparison in tabs two through twelve draws its inputs from here. If a number is wrong in Tab 1, everything downstream is wrong. So before we talk about the exciting stuff — the revenue curves, the break-even timeline, the scenario comparisons — we need to be crystal clear on what this tab says, why it was built this way, and where the risks live. Peanut eSIM is a travel connectivity startup aimed at airline cabin crew. The founding team is Balraj in Dubai handling the technology, finance, and operations side, and Akito in Japan leading brand, marketing, and user experience. The initial target is Emirates cabin crew based in Dubai — a group of roughly 18,000 people who travel to 130 destinations worldwide and need mobile data in every single one of them. The business model is simple: sell eSIM data plans via a mobile app, bundled around a crew-specific workflow, and layer on premium features that generic eSIM providers like Airalo and Holafly cannot replicate. Tab 1 is where every assumption that drives that model is written down and justified. This revision updates the cabin class tier mix to reflect real-world market research benchmarks from Airalo, Holafly, RevenueCat, and crew field research. The numbers that changed are the distribution across tiers — not the prices themselves. Prices remain locked. SECTION 1: THE COLOR KEY The model uses a four-color coding system. Blue cells are hardcoded inputs — raw numbers that a human typed in. Black cells are formula-driven — they calculate automatically from other cells. Green cells are cross-sheet references — they pull from another tab. Yellow cells are key assumptions — numbers that matter most, that the team should review regularly, and that scenario testing should always include. This color discipline sounds like a minor formatting choice but it is actually one of the most important features of the model. In any financial model, the most dangerous errors come from accidentally hardcoding a number that should be a formula, or overwriting a formula with a number and not noticing. The color system makes those mistakes immediately visible. If you see a black cell and you want to change it, you need to understand the formula first. If you see a blue cell and it never changes, someone might have frozen a number that should have been updated. For NotebookLM listeners: when someone on the team refers to a "blue cell" or a "yellow assumption," they mean a specific cell type within this framework. The model was designed so that anyone — including team members who did not build it — can orient themselves within five seconds. SECTION 2: CABIN CLASS RETAIL PRICES This is the heart of Tab 1 and arguably the most strategically interesting section of the whole model. Peanut uses a seven-tier pricing structure. The tiers are named after aircraft cabin classes, which is deliberate — the naming is instantly legible to crew, reflects their daily language, and creates a status ladder that encourages upgrades. Here are the seven tiers: Economy is the entry-level pay-as-you-go plan. It costs $4.99 per gigabyte — charged at 2.5 GB average trip usage — and has zero percent data carry-over. This plan exists specifically to remove the barrier to first use. A crew member who has never heard of Peanut can try it on their next layover without committing to a subscription. The revenue per user is modest, but the acquisition value is high. Economy users who like the product are the primary conversion funnel into the subscription tiers above. Roster Bundle is the scheduled-for-crew subscription at $39.99 per month. It delivers 5 gigabytes and has zero carry-over — use it or lose it. The zero carry-over is intentional: it creates a clean monthly revenue cycle and reduces the complexity of tracking rolling data balances. It also creates upgrade pressure toward the higher subscription tiers, which is exactly what the business wants. Premium Economy costs $12.99 per month and gives 10 gigabytes with 50 percent carry-over. This tier is designed for the crew member who travels moderately — maybe two or three layovers a month — and hates the feeling of wasted data. The 50 percent carry-over means unused gigabytes partially roll forward. Business class sits at $24.99 per month, provides 15 gigabytes of data, and carries over 70 percent. The model marks this as the recommended tier. The recommendation designation is strategic: it influences in-app merchandising. When a user opens the pricing screen, Business class will be visually highlighted with a label or color treatment. At $24.99, it is positioned just below the psychological $30 threshold, and 15 gigabytes is appropriate for moderate cross-region flying — though field research from Firsty shows crew actually need only 3 to 5 gigabytes per layover because hotel WiFi covers the base load. This means Business class is actually comfortable headroom rather than tight provisioning, which is ideal positioning. First class is priced at $49.99 per month, provides 20 gigabytes, and offers 100 percent carry-over capped at a two-month rolling maximum. The two-month cap is an important detail — it prevents a heavy traveler from accumulating a massive data bank over six or twelve months, which would destroy the unit economics. Cockpit Annual is the pilot-focused annual plan, priced at $299 per year. That works out to $24.92 per month equivalent — almost exactly the same as Business monthly. It provides 12 gigabytes per month with a 12 gigabyte lifetime emergency bank and 100 percent carry-over. Pilots operate on predictable rosters, often fly the same routes repeatedly, and tend to be higher earners with a preference for annual subscriptions over monthly billing friction. From a cash flow perspective, annual plans are also extremely valuable to the business — collecting 12 months of revenue upfront dramatically improves the working capital position. According to RevenueCat's State of Subscription Apps 2025, annual subscribers are 2.4 to 3 times more valuable over their lifetime than monthly subscribers. That is a significant structural argument for nudging pilots toward Cockpit Annual. Refuel is the add-on top-up at $5.99 for 1 additional gigabyte. It applies to any user who hits their monthly or annual cap and needs more data mid-layover. Refuel is a high-margin item — the incremental cost to deliver 1 gigabyte is well below the wholesale price, especially in Tier-A geographies — and it captures spending from the exact moment a user is most motivated: they're connected, traveling, and suddenly out of data. Industry benchmarks from telecom add-on research show that top-up attach rates of 25 to 35 percent are achievable for engaged subscriber bases. Why seven tiers rather than three or four? The answer is that crew are not a homogeneous segment. Short-haul crew flying regional routes have completely different data needs from long-haul crew on transoceanic routes. Pilots have different patterns from cabin crew. Monthly travelers have different preferences from occasional travelers. A single plan or even a three-tier structure would either over-serve low-volume users at low prices or under-serve high-volume users with data caps. Seven tiers let the model capture maximum willingness to pay across the full segment. SECTION 3: GEO COST BUCKETS Now we move from the revenue side to the cost side. Delivering eSIM data is not a fixed cost — it varies dramatically by country. The wholesale supplier purchases data capacity from local mobile networks, and the price per gigabyte-day in Japan or Singapore is completely different from the price in Brazil or South Africa. The model handles this through three geographic cost buckets. Tier A covers the cheap countries: Japan, United Kingdom, Singapore, Malaysia, Hong Kong, South Korea, and the United States. These markets have dense mobile infrastructure, intense competition between carriers, and deep wholesale markets. The blended cost for Tier A is $0.97 per gigabyte per day. Tier B covers medium-cost countries: Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Vietnam, and the UAE. These markets have good infrastructure but less wholesale depth, often due to geographic isolation or lower population density. The cost here is $1.50 per gigabyte per day. Tier C covers the expensive markets: India, Brazil, Saudi Arabia, and South Africa. These are either heavily regulated markets, markets with limited wholesale competition, or markets where the roaming data infrastructure requires premium partners. The cost jumps to $5.80 per gigabyte per day — nearly six times the Tier A rate. The model applies a default traffic mix of 60 percent Tier A, 25 percent Tier B, and 15 percent Tier C. This blended mix produces a weighted average cost of $1.83 per gigabyte. The 60/25/15 split reflects the actual Emirates network. Emirates flies heavily to Asia-Pacific and transatlantic routes — Tokyo, Singapore, London, New York — all Tier A destinations. The Tier B and Tier C exposure is real but proportionally smaller. Why does this matter? Because gross margin is not uniform across the user base. A crew member who primarily flies Singapore routes is a high-margin customer. A crew member who regularly lays over in Mumbai or Johannesburg costs significantly more to serve. The blended $1.83 number is a model average — actual margin per user will vary based on their specific route portfolio. The biggest risk in this section is that the 60/25/15 mix is an assumption, not measured data. In year one, before Peanut has actual usage data, the team is flying blind on geo mix. If early adopters happen to be heavy India or Brazil travelers, the actual blended cost could be $2.50 or higher, compressing margins significantly. SECTION 4: RESEARCH BASIS — WHY THESE MIX NUMBERS Before explaining the updated tier mix, it is worth explaining how the prior version of this model was calibrated versus how this version was calibrated. The previous version used intuitive assumptions — Roster Bundle at 35 percent because it seemed like the natural flagship pick. This version uses real market benchmarks to anchor the assumptions, then adjusts them for the specific crew-market context. The key research inputs are as follows. Airalo is the largest eSIM marketplace in the world with approximately 20 million users and reported revenue of $331 million in 2024 (source: Airalo press releases). That implies a blended average revenue per user of $16.55 per year — or roughly $1.38 per month. That is a strikingly low number. It tells us that the dominant behavior pattern for eSIM users globally is pay-per-trip, not monthly subscription. Users buy a plan for a trip, go dark between trips, and buy again the next time they travel. This is the defining insight behind raising the Economy/PAYG share in this model. Holafly is the second major eSIM player, with approximately 15 million eSIMs sold and $200 million in revenue in 2024 (source: Holafly coverage via Travolution). That implies an average order value of $13.33 per eSIM. Again, this is consistent with a heavily transactional, per-trip purchase model rather than recurring monthly subscriptions. Crew data need reality check: field research from Firsty — who conducted extensive surveys of frequent business and crew travelers — found that the actual data need per layover is 3 to 5 gigabytes, not 10 to 15 gigabytes. The reason is that hotel WiFi handles the base data load for most travelers: Netflix, email, messaging. The eSIM is used for transit (airport, cab), checking Google Maps, and mobile-specific tasks. This finding has direct implications for Business class positioning — 15 GB is generous relative to need, meaning the value perception is strong — and for Roster Bundle, which at 5 GB per month may actually be too tight for crew flying four or five routes per month. The same Firsty research found that 53 percent of crew over-buy their current eSIM products and 40 percent under-buy them (source: Firsty field research via LinkedIn). This is a stunning finding. Over half the potential market is currently buying more than they need, and 40 percent are running out of data. That is the positioning goldmine for Peanut's roster-upload feature — the AI-driven auto-sizing that reads the crew roster and recommends the right plan before the month starts. We are not selling data plans; we are selling data confidence. Monthly churn benchmarks: RevenueCat's State of Subscription Apps 2025 reports that the average monthly app churn rate for travel and lifestyle subscription apps is 8 to 12 percent. That is high. It implies that a subscriber acquired in month one has a 44 percent probability of still being subscribed at month six in the worst case. This is why the model applies different churn rates per tier — the tiers with higher price points and more utilitarian value (Business, Cockpit) have structurally lower churn than the commodity tiers. Annual subscription retention advantage: RevenueCat's data shows that annual subscribers in the same apps have a first-year retention rate of approximately 44 percent versus 17 percent for monthly subscribers measured over the same period. This is the mathematical case for the Cockpit Annual plan and for nudging users toward annual billing wherever possible. Refuel attach rate benchmarks: Telecom add-on research consistently shows attach rates of 25 to 35 percent for data top-up products on engaged subscriber bases. Our previous 10 percent assumption was materially too conservative. Updated to 30 percent. SECTION 5: CABIN CLASS MIX — UPDATED This section defines what percentage of the paying user base falls into each pricing tier. This is the section with the most significant changes from the prior version of the model. The updated mix is as follows: Economy/PAYG accounts for 50 percent of users, Roster Bundle 18 percent, Premium Economy 13 percent, Business 8 percent, First 5 percent, Cockpit Annual 3 percent, and Refuel add-on purchases across 30 percent of active subscribers in any given month. Economy at 50 percent: The previous model assumed 30 percent PAYG. That was inconsistent with the Airalo and Holafly benchmark data. When the largest eSIM platform in the world has a blended ARPU of $1.38 per month — which only makes sense if most users are buying one or two trips per year rather than subscribing monthly — then a crew-specific platform launching into the same behavior pool should not assume the majority of users are monthly subscribers from day one. 50 percent Economy acknowledges the real baseline behavior. The conversion rate from Economy to subscriptions becomes the key growth lever. Roster Bundle at 18 percent: The previous assumption was 35 percent. This was the most consequential reduction in the revision. The rationale: Roster Bundle at $39.99 per month is priced near the upper bound of what a Airalo/Holafly user currently pays on a per-trip basis. A user who buys two $15 trip plans per month would pay $30 on a per-trip basis; Roster Bundle asks them to pay $40 with the value proposition being convenience and crew-specific features. That is a real upgrade ask. 18 percent is a more honest estimate of the early subscriber cohort that values the roster-integration feature enough to pay a premium for it. Premium Economy at 13 percent: Minor reduction from 15 percent. This tier is for moderate travelers who want carry-over but not the full Business commitment. 13 percent is consistent with it being a meaningful tier without cannibalizing Business. Business at 8 percent: Reduced from 12 percent. The Firsty data showing crew only need 3 to 5 GB per layover suggests that a 15 GB pool at $24.99 sounds generous but may be over-provisioned relative to what a first-time subscriber thinks they need. Early cohorts will likely need to experience one or two months of usage before they feel comfortable committing to a $25/month plan. 8 percent is an honest early-adopter figure that should grow as the product matures. First at 5 percent: Unchanged. This is the power user tier for long-haul frequent travelers who genuinely need 20+ GB of data. 5 percent of a crew base is realistic. Cockpit at 3 percent: Slightly increased from 2 percent, recognizing the structural advantages of annual billing and the pilot-specific value proposition. Pilots as a cohort are higher-income, higher-routine users who are natural annual plan buyers. Refuel at 30 percent attach: Increased from 10 percent based on the telecom benchmark data (25 to 35 percent industry range) and the recognition that crew are exactly the kind of engaged, mid-activity user who converts on in-app top-up prompts. When you are at an airport taxi queue in Tokyo and your data runs out, you are not opening a browser comparison engine — you tap Refuel. These six tiers plus the Refuel attach rate must always sum to a coherent picture of the user base. The mix drives blended revenue per user per month, which is the single most important top-line metric in the model. SECTION 6: CHURN RATES BY TIER This section was previously housed in Tab 8 but is surfaced here because it is a critical assumption that flows into cohort modeling. Per-tier monthly churn rates: Economy 18 percent, Roster Bundle 10 percent, Premium Economy 10 percent, Business 9 percent, First 8 percent, Cockpit Annual 0.6 percent per month (approximately 7 percent per year, consistent with the RevenueCat 44 percent first-year annual subscriber retention benchmark). Economy's 18 percent monthly churn is high because PAYG users are transactional by definition — they activate for a trip and go dormant. The monthly retention figure is misleading in isolation; what matters for Economy is the reactivation rate, which is the proportion of dormant PAYG users who make another purchase within 90 days. Cockpit's 0.6 percent monthly churn is the structural floor of the model and the most valuable cohort per dollar of acquisition cost. An annual subscriber who renews has a lifetime value that is 2.4 to 3 times a monthly subscriber at the same price point, per RevenueCat benchmarks. SECTION 7: OPERATING COST PER USER PER MONTH The final section of Tab 1 breaks down the non-data operating cost to serve a single user for one month. Five line items total $1.10 per user per month. This figure is unchanged from the prior model version. Cloud and API infrastructure costs $0.15 per user. This covers the backend systems — servers, databases, the eSIM provisioning API calls, and developer tooling. At early scale, this is likely higher per user due to fixed infrastructure overhead that gets amortized as volume grows. Payment processing costs $0.50 per user. This is the largest single line item in opex and reflects the reality of operating in the payments space. Stripe and equivalent processors charge around 2.9 percent plus $0.30 per transaction. The $0.50 assumption implies an average transaction value around $8. As users migrate to higher subscription tiers, the percentage cost comes down but the dollar amount per transaction goes up. This line item deserves careful monitoring. Customer support costs $0.30 per user per month. eSIM installation is the primary support trigger — crew who have never installed an eSIM before will need help. The model assumes a mix of AI-assisted support via an in-app chat agent and some human escalation. As the AI support layer matures, this number should decline. The risk is that if eSIM installation issues are more common than expected, the actual support cost per user could run two or three times the modeled figure. SMS and OTP authentication costs $0.05 per user. This is modest and well-understood — these are fixed-rate bulk SMS costs for verification messages. Hosting and developer tools cost $0.10 per user. This covers GitHub, CI/CD, monitoring tools, and similar developer infrastructure at scale. The total of $1.10 per user per month is intentionally lean. It is achievable at meaningful scale — say, 500 users or more — but in the first three to six months while the user base is small, the per-user cost will effectively be higher because fixed costs are spread over a tiny base. WHAT TAB 1 DRIVES DOWNSTREAM Every tab in the model that follows reads from Tab 1. The revenue model in Tab 2 uses the cabin class prices, the user mix percentages, and the Refuel add-on rate to project monthly revenue. The cost of goods tab uses the geo bucket costs and the blended $1.83 rate to calculate gross margin. The operating cost tabs apply the $1.10 per-user formula across the user count projections. The scenario analysis tabs swap in alternative values for key yellow assumptions — like the cabin class mix or the geo cost blend — and show the impact on EBITDA. Tab 1 is essentially the DNA of the model. Change one assumption here, and you instantly see the downstream effect everywhere else. That's the power of building a model this way — it's a living document, not a static spreadsheet. KEY RISKS AND THINGS TO WATCH First risk: the wholesale supplier cost structure. The current model uses costs from a single wholesale supplier who is a sub-reseller. This means Peanut is not buying data capacity at the best available market rate. The actual margin improvement available by moving to a direct relationship with a top-tier eSIM wholesale provider could be substantial — potentially reducing data costs by 30 to 50 percent. The model currently reflects conservative assumptions, but the team should actively pursue direct supplier relationships in parallel with growth. Second risk: the geo mix assumption. The 60/25/15 Tier-A/B/C split will not be known with certainty until the app is live and usage data is flowing. If early crew users are heavy Tier-C destination travelers, margins compress immediately. The team should track actual usage by geography from day one and update Tab 1 quarterly. Third risk: Economy conversion rate. With 50 percent of the user base in PAYG/Economy, the revenue model is highly sensitive to how quickly those users convert to monthly subscriptions. If conversion is below 15 percent in the first 60 days, the blended ARPU will lag the model. Build conversion tracking from day one. Fourth risk: Roster Bundle adoption. The reduction from 35 to 18 percent is a more honest assumption, but it also means Roster Bundle is no longer the model's primary revenue anchor. If the crew-specific roster-upload workflow resonates strongly and adoption tracks closer to 25 percent, the model improves materially. Fifth risk: Refuel execution. Moving from 10 to 30 percent attach is a 3x improvement in a single line item. That only happens if the in-app top-up prompt is well-designed, well-timed, and converts. If Refuel stays at 10 percent because the UX is poor, the model loses a meaningful revenue line. Sixth risk: support costs at launch. Early-stage eSIM products have higher support needs than steady-state products. The $0.30 per user per month figure is a steady-state assumption. The actual first-month support cost per user could be three to five times higher. Key Risks and Things to Watch

Wholesale supplier cost structure. The current model reflects costs from a sub-reseller, not a direct tier-1 wholesale relationship. A direct deal could reduce data costs by 30–50%. The team should pursue this actively in parallel with growth — it is the single largest margin lever in the model.

Geo mix assumption. The 60/25/15 Tier-A/B/C split will not be known until the app is live. Track actual usage by geography from day one and update Tab 1 quarterly. A swing to 25% Tier-C traffic could cut gross margin significantly.

Economy conversion rate. With 50% of users in PAYG, the model is highly sensitive to how quickly they convert to monthly subscriptions. If conversion is below 15% in the first 60 days, blended ARPU will lag. Build conversion tracking from day one.

Refuel execution. Moving from 10% to 30% attach is a 3× improvement. That only happens with well-designed, well-timed in-app prompts. If Refuel stays at 10% due to poor UX, the model loses a meaningful revenue line.

Payment processing at low ticket values. If average transaction value is lower than assumed — because Economy plans at $4.99/GB dominate early — the processing cost as a percentage of revenue climbs. Monitor average order value monthly.

Support costs at launch. Early eSIM products have higher support needs than steady-state products. The $0.30/user figure is mature-stage. Budget for 3–5× that number in month one and two.

Closing

Tab 1 is designed to be a living document. The team reviews it quarterly, or whenever a major assumption changes — a new supplier agreement, a pricing update, a new geographic market. The color-coded system means anyone can open the file, immediately see which cells are inputs versus formulas, and know exactly what to update when reality diverges from projection.

The goal is not precision — startup financials are never precise. The goal is a structured framework where the team can test any assumption in minutes and get an honest answer about how it flows through to the bottom line. That framework starts here, in Tab 1.
