1. What Tab 4 Does

Tab 4 answers a single question: given any wholesale cost per GB, what retail price produces a target margin? It works in three directions:

  1. Verify current margins — input $1.34/GB blended, see that $17.99 Premium produces 70% margin. This is the verification use case, and it is relevant now.
  2. Set prices for a new wholesale deal — input negotiated rate, see what margin each existing price produces. Decides whether to drop prices (pass on savings) or hold prices (pocket margin improvement). Current recommendation: pocket.
  3. Model "what if we charged differently" — explore hypothetical retail prices. This is the most dangerous use case — it requires both a new wholesale rate AND new retail price validation before acting.

The tab is not a market-positioning tool. It does not look at competitor prices. It does not model demand elasticity. It is pure cost-based margin math.

Why $0.30 and $0.20 are estimates, not facts

These figures were derived from general eSIM wholesale pricing research, not from any supplier quote. Volume discount curves vary dramatically across suppliers. The actual negotiated rate at 5K users could be anywhere from $0.50 to $0.25/GB depending on commitment volume, exclusivity, and carrier mix. The only thing Tab 4 guarantees is that any improvement from $1.34 will produce dramatic margin gains — the exact number is unknown until we have the conversation.

3. Pricing Matrix: Breakeven to 70% Margin

For each tier, there are 4 margin reference points:

  • Break-even price = COGS only (covers wholesale cost, nothing else)
  • 30% margin = COGS / 0.7 (viable but thin — covers some OPEX)
  • 50% margin = COGS / 0.5 (healthy — sector-standard SaaS range)
  • 70% margin = COGS / 0.3 (where we are today on most tiers)

First Suite is the most sensitive tier

At $1.34/GB and 25 GB pool, First Suite COGS is $33.50 — nearly half of the $69.99 retail price. This is why First Suite shows only 52% margin at 60% consumption. For a 70% margin target, First Suite would need to retail at $111.67 — far above any competitor equivalent. The realistic path: cut the pool to 18 GB (COGS $24.12, 65% margin at current retail) or raise price to $79.99 (71% margin at 25 GB). This is a Tab 1 / Tab 3 fix, not a Tab 4 fix.

4. Worked Example: Aiko on Premium Economy

Aiko is a Premium Economy subscriber. She pays $17.99/month for 4 GB of pooled data. This is the most common subscription tier and the clearest illustration of what procurement improvement looks like.

6. The Key Insight

Single most important takeaway
Pricing is not the lever. Supplier negotiation is.

Our current 70% gross margins are already strong. The next 23 percentage points of margin improvement do not come from a UI pricing change, a promotion, or a product decision. They come from a single supplier conversation that requires ~5,000 users and a credible alternative in hand. This is why Tab 15 (Procurement Options) is more operationally urgent than Tab 4.

What this means for retail pricing today

Do not touch retail prices to acquire users. The math does not support it:

Rejected: drop Premium to $14.99 "to grow faster"

This costs $3.00/user/month in lost margin immediately. At 500 users, that is $1,500/month permanently lost. User growth from a price drop is uncertain; margin loss is guaranteed. And re-raising prices after a promotional period creates churn. The model does not recover.

Correct approach: hold retail, grow word-of-mouth, negotiate later

Keep $17.99 Premium, $29.99 Business. Every user acquired at current prices has the full 70% margin. Once volume reaches ~5K and we negotiate, the same users are now producing 93% margin. The improvement is automatic, retroactive, and permanent.

7. When This Tab Becomes Active

Four specific conditions must all be true before Tab 4 transitions from parked to active decision tool:

  1. ~5,000 active users
    This is the minimum volume needed to have negotiating credibility. Below 5K, suppliers have no incentive to offer meaningful discounts. The number is approximate — some suppliers move at 2K, some require 10K. Assess at 3K.
  2. Alternative supplier identified
    A credible alternative in hand gives us BATNA (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement). Without a competing quote, we have no leverage. This is Step 1 of Tab 15's Phase 2 roadmap.
  3. Supplier meeting scheduled
    The meeting itself is the trigger. Bring Tab 4 as a scenario simulator in the meeting. Show that at their proposed rate, our new margin target, and our user volume, here is what the math produces.
  4. Actual quoted rate received
    Only then does the tab go live. Replace Scenario B's $0.30/GB with the real quoted number. Run the matrix. Decide whether to hold retail prices (pocket margin) or pass some savings to users (grow faster). Current recommendation: pocket.
