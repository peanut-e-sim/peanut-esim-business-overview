Quick Context for New Claude Instances

Balraj Singh Kalra (Dubai, UAE) and Akito (Japan-based co-founder) are building a travel eSIM brand called Peanut eSIM (brand name still TBD as of 2026-05-24). The core thesis: Emirates cabin crew complain in Facebook groups about which eSIM to use — Balraj and Akito spotted that pain and decided to build a crew-specific eSIM app instead of competing in the generic travel-eSIM mass market.

Section 1

The Thesis

The project began on 2026-05-21 when Balraj told Claude: "I'm starting a new project and I would really want you to understand the context and the details about my project." He described that Akito (initially transcribed as "Hikito" — corrected early in the session) had met a business owner in Thailand who runs an eSIM aggregator. Akito proposed: let's white-label this, brand it for the UAE market, target Emirates crew as seed users.

The founding insight

The demand signal was specific: repeated "which eSIM should I use?" threads in Emirates crew Facebook groups. Balraj and Akito both have UAE residency and network access into Emirates crew communities. The observation: this isn't just a market to serve, it's a closed community to build a platform for.

"The whole idea came from seeing crew discussion groups... I have connections with a lot of the crew members from Emirates we can reach out to them and have them as users for our eSIM because they are the ones who travel a lot for work and leisure." — Balraj, 2026-05-21

What makes it defensible

MoatWhy it works
Closed network gatingCrew-only verification creates quality + exclusivity. Same mechanic as the direct competitor (crew-only eSIM brand).
Community network effectOnce N crew are on the app, the (N+1)th has more reason to join
Schedule-upload featureProprietary UX that requires OCR + crew-specific logic; can't be copied without crew knowledge
Crew-curated travel intelRestaurants, hotels, recs — data that can only be collected from crew, in a crew-only space

Long-term expansion arc

Phase-by-phase airline expansion: Emirates (v1) → Etihad (v2) → JAL/ANA (v3) → other carriers. Each airline unlocked after reaching critical crew mass at the previous one. Revenue stages: eSIM data sales → subscriptions → merchant referrals on community recommendations.

Section 3

The Supplier Story

The supply-chain investigation is the most technically evolved thread in the project. It went through three distinct theories over four days.

Starting point: the wholesale supplier (Akito's contact)

The initial wholesale partner is a Japan-based brand that started as pocket-WiFi rental for Japanese outbound tourists and now offers eSIM reseller packages. The key data: their wholesale price list contained 9,091 SKUs, priced in CNY (Chinese Yuan, not Thai Baht), with a perfectly flat ~40% margin across all rows.

"The flat 40% is the textbook signature of a Tier-3 sub-reseller." — Claude, 2026-05-21

The margin math that revealed the problem

Cost itemUSA 10GB/7d via Apple IAP at $6.04 retail
Wholesale supplier settlement−$3.61
Apple IAP 30%−$1.81
Stripe 3%−$0.18
Actual net profit$0.44 (7%)
Same sale via direct Tier-1 (estimated)~$1.55 (26%)

Theory 1: Alternative aggregator as upstream (partially refuted)

A Deep Research report predicted the CNY pricing was "almost certainly" the signature of a specific Chinese aggregator. Balraj opened an account and downloaded their price file. When compared against the wholesale supplier's rates, the wholesale supplier was cheaper on several routes (Japan: $2.57 vs $2.70; USA: $2.64 vs $3.31). The theory was substantially weakened. Claude acknowledged explicitly: "The data tells a different story than the Deep Research predicted."

Theory 2: Global WiFi / Vision Inc. (Akito's intel, later partially refuted)

On 2026-05-22, Balraj relayed Akito's intel: "Global WiFi is the supplier for [wholesale partner] from Japan." This was filed to memory and shaped strategy for two days. A recommendation was drafted to eventually pursue Vision Inc. / Global WiFi directly for better margin.

The final verified answer (2026-05-24)

A Researcher sub-agent analyzed the wholesale supplier's own price file at the column level. In the eSIM product sheet, a column identifying the matching carrier revealed a single Tier-1 aggregator on 5,294 rows. Zero mentions of Vision Inc. anywhere in the eSIM section.

Confirmed supply chain (2026-05-24): 
MNOs (carriers) → Tier-1 aggregator (HK) → wholesale supplier (~40% markup) → Peanut eSIM
Vision Inc. / Global WiFi IS the upstream for the wholesale supplier's pocket-WiFi rental business — but that is a separate product line. Akito's intel was correct but applied to the wrong product.

BD contact for Tier-1 aggregator

A BD contact at the Singapore office was identified. Direct outreach initiated 2026-05-24. Going direct would recover approximately $1.11 per sale at typical plan sizes, or ~$13,320/year at 1,000 sales/month.

The Apple tax problem

Apple takes 30% of all in-app purchases. This destroys margin at Tier-3 wholesale rates. The recommended solution (Netflix/Spotify model): first purchase on the web (keeps 100% margin), app handles activation/management only. Top-ups inside the app go through IAP — smaller, less frequent transactions. Decision 01 on Open Discussion; not yet locked.

Section 5

The Wedge Feature (Schedule Upload)

The day-1 competitive differentiator went through a pivot. The original lean was "better price" — undercut Airalo and the direct competitor by 10-20%. This changed after competitive research revealed the direct competitor (crew-only eSIM brand, ~5K users, German company) is already at $1.50/GB on most major destinations, competitive with Airalo. Price alone won't move the needle.

"The real wedge is the schedule-upload smart-plan feature. [The competitor] doesn't have it, their solo team can't easily build it, and it's the thing that makes Peanut feel made-for-this-specific-job." — Claude, after Crew SIM research, 2026-05-22

New positioning: "Built around your roster, by people who know your airline."

This was independently confirmed by the Airalo/Holafly competitor teardown, which listed "Calendar-first search, not country-first" as move #6 in its 12 actionable recommendations — reaching the same conclusion from a different direction.

Technical spec for the OCR engine

Based on two real Crew Pulse screenshots (Emirates' official crew scheduling app) shared by Balraj — monthly calendar grid, 7 columns Mon-Sun, 3-letter IATA codes, color-coded pairing cells (purple = active trip).

Smart-plan engine stepLogic
1. Parse screenshotOCR extracts month/year + list of (date, airport_code, trip_type) tuples
2. Drop home airport legsEmirates crew are DXB-based — home legs don't need eSIM
3. Group into layoversOutbound → destination → return to home
4. Estimate data needs2 GB for 24h layover, 5 GB for 36h, 10 GB for longer
5. Match to catalogAggregator API lookup per destination
6. Output bundleFull month, total price, savings vs à la carte
Privacy decision (Decision 09, lean confirmed): OCR runs on-device only. Roster data reveals Emirates operational information. Never upload raw roster screenshots to Peanut servers.
Section 6

The Moat — Community Features

Three features spec'd by Balraj on 2026-05-22. All filed to memory, all awaiting Phase 2 Item 5 integration into the roadmap.

Crew Recs — the Michelin layer

Contribution: Crew at a restaurant → opens it in Safari → iOS Share Sheet → Peanut eSIM appears as target → modal with URL prefilled → add category, airplane-icon rating, photos, notes → posted to crew database.

Discovery: Browse by country/city → Crew Recommendations section → filter by category → see photos + ratings + crew notes.

Reward mechanic: Like-only (no dislikes — prevents Yelp-style degradation). Likes accumulate as Peanut Points. 100 likes = 1 GB free data redeemed. Top contributors get badges + multipliers. Free data costs ~$0.50/GB wholesale — effectively a marketing expense.

Crew Accommodation

Emirates assigns crew to specific hotels at each layover city. Crew can't choose the hotel but can prepare differently with intel. Feature: per-hotel amenity checklist (dryer, iron, closet, quiet rooms, blackout curtains, gym, pool, WiFi, airport transport distance) + crew-contributed photos + auto-generated pre-trip checklists.

AI Support Tab

Persistent Support tab in the app. AI chat for: eSIM install help, top-up questions, connectivity issues, refunds. AI-first (90% of questions), human escalation for billing disputes. The direct competitor uses email/ticket only; crew work red-eyes — 8-hour reply times are unacceptable.

Tech stack recommendation: Custom Claude/OpenAI API for v1 (~$0.10-0.50/conversation, full control). Migrate to Intercom Fin at scale (Phase 5+).
