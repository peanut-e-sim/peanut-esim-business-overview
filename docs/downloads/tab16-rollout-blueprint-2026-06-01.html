1. Why this tab matters

All the previous tabs show the model as a steady-state snapshot: "at 1,000 users, here is the math." Tab 16 answers the harder question: how long until 1,000 users? When do we stop burning cash? What is the path?

Investors, cofounders, and Balraj himself need to know: "I can sustain this for X months before it has to work." Tab 16 makes that commitment visible. It also shows the steepness of the S-curve — growth is slow at first (word-of-mouth compounds slowly), then accelerates in the crew social network effect.

3. The discussion we had — four bites

Bite 1 — What the tab actually does: adding TIME

The key insight from this walkthrough: tabs 3-14 are all timeless math ("per user, per month, in perpetuity"). Tab 16 is the first tab that adds a time axis. It forces an answer to: how fast does this grow? The S-curve shape was chosen deliberately — not a straight line (too optimistic at launch, too pessimistic at scale) but a slow start accelerating into a compound social growth effect as crew recommend to crew.

We discussed whether the S-curve was realistic. Consensus: the launch is genuinely slow (50 users = Akito's personal network and early beta) because Emirates crew travel in closed social circles. Once they trust the product, referrals compound within those circles. The 3-5x monthly growth rates in M1-M6 are aggressive but defensible if Akito is actively pushing.

Bite 2 — Milestone walkthrough

We walked through each milestone and stress-tested whether the user numbers behind them were achievable in the timeframe.

  • M1 = 50 users: Launch milestone. This is literally Akito's immediate crew network — people he can WhatsApp personally. Achievable with zero marketing spend.
  • M3 = First $1,000/month: Requires ~130 users at $17.52 ARPU. Doable within 3 months if word spreads to 2-3 crew groups beyond Akito's immediate contacts.
  • M6 = First $5,000/month + Ramen-covered: 450 users. "Ramen-covered" = the business covers founders' basic living expenses. The definition is intentionally loose — it depends on what Balraj and Akito consider "ramen."
  • M11-12 = TRUE breakeven: The exact month depends heavily on tier mix (see Tab 17). With Steady State mix (~$17.52 ARPU), breakeven at roughly 700-800 users. At 1,800 users by M11, we are well past breakeven — the model shows meaningful profit accumulation by this point.
  • M13 = $25K/month net: This was the first "feels like a real business" number Balraj mentioned. 2,400 users × $17.52 ARPU − $11K opex − COGS = roughly $25K net. Achieved by M13 in base case.
  • M24 = $50K/month net: The 24-month target. 5,000 users. This is where the financial model demonstrates the business is self-sustaining and has real exit optionality.

Bite 3 — When the business starts working

The exact breakeven month was debated. The $11K/month fixed burn figure is a placeholder — there is no actual spend yet as of 2026-06-01. The number was derived from Tab 1 (operating cost structure) as a modeling assumption. Actual spend may be materially lower (no staff yet, mostly Stripe fees + cloud + App Store) or higher (if Akito needs a salary sooner than expected).

Key finding from the discussion: the word-of-mouth growth model means breakeven comes BEFORE we have proved the conversion funnel. At M7-M8, we should have enough users to see real tier upgrade data — but by the time we see it, we're already near breakeven. There is almost no window where the business is burning cash AND we have useful data to change course. The model is forgiving.

Bite 4 — Sensitivity (ARPU and churn)

We tested two sensitivity levers: ARPU and churn.

  • ARPU is the biggest lever: At Month 24 (5,000 users), a $1 change in blended ARPU = $5,000/month impact. The ARPU range from Behavior Sensitivity (Tab 17) is $14.55 (Launch Reality) to $21.48 (Mature Crew). That's a $6.93 spread = potential $34,650/month swing at M24. The tier conversion story is the biggest profit lever in the entire model.
  • Churn pressure — the dangerous assumption: The base model assumes 5% monthly churn across all tiers (blended). The industry average for consumer travel apps is 8-12%. We are betting on the crew-product thesis: crew have a recurring need (monthly flights), they form habits with apps they trust, and the social network effect within crew circles reduces churn. If this thesis is wrong and churn goes to industry average (10%), the breakeven month moves from M11-12 to M15-16.

Why ARPU is the biggest sensitivity lever

At 5,000 users, a $1 change in blended ARPU = $5,000/month change in revenue. The ARPU spread from Behavior Sensitivity is $6.93 ($21.48 − $14.55). That is a potential $34,650/month swing. The single most impactful thing Akito and Balraj can do for Month 24 profitability is convert Economy users to subscription tiers. Everything else is secondary.

The dangerous churn assumption

The base model uses 5% blended monthly churn. The industry average for consumer travel-adjacent apps is 8-12%. We are making a specific bet: that crew-product fit is strong enough to hold churn at half the industry average. The evidence for this bet: (1) crew have monthly recurring data needs, (2) the social group effect creates social switching cost, (3) the Roster Bundle auto-arming feature creates high switching friction. If any of these fail, churn reverts to industry average and the growth model becomes much harder. This is the most important assumption to validate in the first 6 months.

8. Strategic takeaway

Three things to take from Tab 16:

  1. The first 6 months are all about proving the churn assumption. If M6 churn data shows 5-6%, the model is on track. If it shows 15%+, the crew-product thesis needs immediate adjustment (better onboarding, better retention features, re-examination of who is being acquired).
  2. ARPU improvement is the main dial. The difference between Launch Reality ARPU ($14.55) and Steady State ARPU ($17.52) is worth $14,850/month at 5K users. That $14K/month difference comes entirely from onboarding better and converting Economy users to subscriptions. This is a product and marketing problem, not a pricing problem.
  3. The opex placeholder needs a real number before M3. Balraj needs to decide: what are the actual monthly costs? Stripe fees alone at 1,000 users = ~$600. Cloud at launch = sub-$500. The model's $11K/mo is anchored to a future state with staff. Until then, actual burn is much lower — which means breakeven comes significantly earlier than M11-12.
