Part 2 · UAE regulatory enforcement — Airalo case study + Plan B

The block is real, ongoing, and has no formal resolution

Research confirmed that multiple travel eSIM providers (including the market leader) experienced network-level blocking in the UAE. This is not a technical outage — it is silent ISP-level enforcement by the UAE's two licensed operators, consistent with the TDRA's regulatory framework requiring that telecommunications services be provided only by UAE licensees or their authorized agents.

Timeline

April 2024
First confirmed blocks. Travel eSIM apps and websites become unreachable on Etisalat connections. DNS + IP level filtering. Multiple providers affected simultaneously. No announcement from the UAE regulator. No due process. No appeal mechanism.
April 2025
Repeat enforcement confirmed. Same pattern documented by travellers — not a temporary outage. Blocks appear persistent across operator network refreshes. Providers publish manual workarounds (install eSIM before departure, use WiFi).
March 2026
Regional spread. Kuwait suspends eSIM sales entirely. Turkey formally blocks 8 eSIM providers by name (July 2025). India removed eSIM apps from app stores (Jan 2024). The UAE remains the most directly relevant risk given CrewLink's seed cohort.
May 2026
No resolution. Airalo's last public statement described the UAE situation as "circumstances we are working relentlessly to solve." No formal unblock announced. Blocks assumed ongoing.
Why this matters for CrewLink: Emirates crew are UAE residents. They buy before departure while on UAE networks. A block would affect plan purchases, top-ups, and potentially profile re-downloads — hitting the seed cohort hardest. The enforcement mechanism is silent (no warning, no appeal), and it tracks commercial traction — providers get blocked when they grow large enough to be noticed.

Plan B — Decision tree for day the block arrives

If CrewLink becomes unreachable on UAE networks

Days 0–3 · Assess
Network-level block vs app store removal
Network block (ISP filter): lower severity — workarounds work immediately. App store removal: critical — requires separate escalation track with Apple/Google. Confirm which operators are blocking (Etisalat, du, or both). Most likely scenario is network-only.
Days 1–7 · Immediate mitigations
Activate offline-first flows
Network block only: Push in-app alert ("Use WiFi or VPN to access CrewLink"). Enable manual QR code activation (bypasses API endpoint calls). Crew WhatsApp/Telegram group: eSIM QR codes can be shared peer-to-peer with no app needed. PWA fallback: crewlink.app accessible via Safari on WiFi — not subject to TDRA enforcement.

App store removal too: Android APK sideload via WhatsApp. iOS TestFlight distribution to verified crew. PWA becomes primary interface.
Weeks 2–4 · Structural response
Legal + supplier + user base diversification
Engage UAE telecom lawyer (Al Tamimi & Company specialises in TDRA matters). Evaluate whether a UAE Free Zone entity (DIFC or DMCC) creates legal basis to operate under a TDRA license. Shift supplier routing — ensure alternative API endpoint not blocked. Accelerate Etihad/Qatar crew expansion to reduce UAE-resident concentration.
Month 2+ · Strategic options
Three long-term paths
Option A — MVNO sub-agreement with e& or du: Becomes a licensed agent. ~$50–100K set-up + revenue share. Expensive but permanent.

Option B — Operate from outside UAE, crew on WiFi/VPN: Emirates crew are experienced VPN users. Practical for 6–12 months.

Option C — Japan-first pivot (JAL/ANA): No TDRA risk. Akito's natural network. Strong eSIM demand from Japan crew.
Pre-emptive move (do now, costs nothing): Add to onboarding: "Always download your eSIM plan before your trip, while on WiFi." Framed as performance best practice. Makes a UAE network block a non-event for any user who followed the instruction. Recommend implementing before v1 launch.
