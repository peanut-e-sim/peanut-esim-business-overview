1. Why this tab matters

Tabs 1 through 13 build the per-user machine — what we charge, what it costs us, what we keep per user per month. Tab 14 is the first tab that asks: multiply that by N users — what does the whole business look like?

The answer tells you the win condition. If the answer at 1,000 users is $150K/yr, and you know word-of-mouth can get you to 1,000 users in 12-18 months, you know the business "works" well before needing outside capital. If the answer at 100K users is $15M/yr, you know what the prize is at full scale.

Three things make this tab interesting beyond the simple multiplication:

  • The blended $12.48 figure is not a given — it is the product of a specific tier mix assumption. Change the mix and you change the number.
  • The model holds the mix flat across all scale scenarios. That is a deliberate conservative assumption, but it understates the realistic endgame by $3.2M/yr.
  • The path from 5K to 20K users is structurally different from 0 to 5K — it requires expanding beyond Emirates crew entirely.

3.2 The hidden assumption — tier mix evolves with scale

What we discussed

The model holds $12.48 flat across all 6 scenarios. We identified this as a deliberate conservative assumption that is mathematically correct for a base case, but is likely wrong directionally. Real user cohorts start Economy-heavy (Beta) and mature toward higher tiers over time. Early adopters tend to be experimental; loyal users climb the ladder.

The key insight: if tier mix evolves toward subscriptions as the platform matures, the model understates the endgame. At 100K users with the realistic mature mix, the annual gross is $18.2M, not $15M — a $3.2M/yr gap.

3.3 What drives tier mix evolution — the 7 nudges

Tier mix does not evolve by accident. It evolves because in-app nudges fire at the right moment and make the math undeniable. We identified 7 specific triggers during this session. They are detailed in Section 5. The core insight: the product team that builds these nudges unlocks the $3.2M/yr gap. The product team that doesn't build them leaves the model flat at $15M.

3.4 The OPEX placeholder discussion

What we confirmed

The Tab 1 OPEX line carries $1.80/user/mo (Stripe fees $1.20 + cloud $0.15 + support $0.30 + SMS $0.05 + hosting $0.10). This is a modeled estimate, not a real expense line. As of 2026-06-02 none of these costs exist — the app is not live, no real Stripe transactions, no real AWS bill.

The decision: present gross = net in all real-world discussions. Use the $1.80/user model for stress-testing only. Never quote the net-after-placeholder-OPEX number as if it is the real profit.

4. Yuki's 12-month journey — the canonical progression story

Yuki is a new Emirates cabin crew member. She signs up in January on Economy (PAYG). By October she is on Business. By end of year she is generating $29.99/month — six times her January revenue — with zero additional acquisition cost. This is the core narrative of how the tier ladder creates value.

Yuki's 12-month summary:
  • Total spend across 12 months: $239.26
  • If she had stayed Economy all year (avg 2 top-ups/mo): approximately $60/yr
  • Ladder value over Economy baseline: +$179/yr — same user, zero extra acquisition cost

5. The 7 ladder nudges — product mechanism for tier progression

These are in-app triggers. Each fires at a specific behavioral moment and presents a math-first case for upgrading. None are forced, none are deceptive, all are reversible. The philosophy: show the user that the upgrade is obviously in their financial interest, then let them decide.

# Trigger Prompt shown to user Tier transition Why it works
1 After 3rd refuel ($14.97 cumulative PAYG spend) "You've spent $14.97 on top-ups. Premium is $17.99 for 10 GB. Switch?" Economy → Premium The math is undeniable. User has proven they will spend the money. $3.02 more = 10x the data.
2 After roster sync upload (6+ flights detected) "You fly 6x this month. Roster Bundle = $23.94 vs $29.94 PAYG. Save?" Economy → Roster Bundle Behavior data triggers it. We know their schedule from the roster. Hyper-personalized.
3 Hit 80% of monthly data pool "You're nearly out of data. Refuel now or upgrade to Business for 15 GB?" Premium → Business Pain in the moment. The user is already experiencing the constraint and is most receptive.
4 3-month subscriber anniversary "You've been flying with us 3 months. Annual Cockpit = $299 — save $360 vs monthly. Ready?" Business → Cockpit annual Proven loyal user. 3 months shows intention. Annual prepay = day-1 cash flow for us.
5 First long-haul flight detected in roster "Long-haul flight detected. First plan covers 50 GB pool — enough for the whole trip." Premium → First Route-triggered from roster data. Relevant at exactly the right moment.
6 Senior crew / Purser badge detected in profile "Senior crew? First Suite includes layover concierge access and a status badge." First → First Suite Status-triggered. Feels like a recognition, not a sales pitch. Identity-aligned.
7 Year 1 renewal (30 days before Cockpit annual expires) "Renew Cockpit for Year 2? Lock in your price + 5% loyalty credit." Retention — Cockpit → Cockpit Year 2 Anniversary moment. Loyalty credit is cheap; retention at year 2 is highly valuable.

Anti-patterns — what we explicitly decided NOT to do

  • No "upgrade!" banners everywhere — users mute them, they erode trust
  • No hidden cost comparison — honesty about the math is a differentiator vs competitors
  • No forced upgrade after X days — cabin crew hate forced anything (they operate on strict schedules already)
  • No charge for downgrades — one-way ratchet by choice only. A user who tries Business and goes back to Premium should be able to do so freely.

6. The 5K to 20K jump — the hardest growth phase

What we discussed

Emirates Airlines employs approximately 22,000 cabin crew. At 25% penetration — which is an aggressive but achievable target via word-of-mouth in a close-knit professional community — that gives us approximately 5,500 users. The Month 24 target is 5,000 users, which aligns with this ceiling.

Getting to 20,000 users means the Emirates ceiling is not enough. We must expand to other airlines: Etihad, JAL, ANA, Qatar Airways, potentially Singapore Airlines and Cathay Pacific. Each airline represents a separate word-of-mouth network, a different cultural onboarding dynamic, and potentially a new partnership relationship.

The core tension: from 0 to 5K, the product markets itself through Emirates crew room conversations and Instagram. From 5K to 20K, we need a new acquisition engine and a new brand positioning — "not just Emirates eSIM, but Crew Universal eSIM." That is a different product story to tell.

The strategic challenge at 5K: The model shows the math as linear — just add more users and revenue scales. The actual difficulty is acquiring the next airline cohort. Etihad crew operate differently from Emirates crew. JAL/ANA crew likely require Japanese-language onboarding (Akito is the right person to lead this). Each airline adds a non-trivial partnership overhead that is not modeled in Tab 14.

7. The honest flags

Flag 1 — OPEX is a placeholder, not real cost

The model's $1.80/user/month OPEX line is a modeled estimate covering Stripe fees, AWS, support amortization, SMS costs, and dev tools. As of launch day there is literally $0 of this being spent. The model shows a "Monthly Net Profit" figure that deducts this placeholder. Do not cite that net figure in early-stage conversations — cite gross, which equals real net today.

Flag 2 — Linear scaling is optimistic beyond 5K

The model multiplies one blended per-user number across all scale steps. In reality, scaling from 5K to 20K requires airline expansion costs (partnership development, localization, new onboarding flows) that are not captured anywhere in the model. The $249K/mo at 20K users is gross data revenue — it does not include any business development cost to acquire the Etihad, JAL, or ANA partnerships.

Flag 3 — Tier mix evolution is modeled as directional, not predictive

The "realistic tier mix evolution" table in Section 3.2 is the authors' estimate of how user behavior changes as a platform matures. It is consistent with general SaaS patterns but has zero validation for crew-specific eSIM behavior. The 7 nudges are the mechanism — but whether Nudge #1 achieves a 10% conversion rate or a 25% conversion rate is unknown until A/B testing is live.

8. Strategic takeaway

Tab 14 is the simplest tab to read and the hardest to act on. The math is clean: users × $12.48 = total gross. The hard part is getting to each user count milestone.
