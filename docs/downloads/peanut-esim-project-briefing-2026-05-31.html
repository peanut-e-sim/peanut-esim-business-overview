2. Elevator pitch & business model

Peanut eSIM (working name; brand still TBD from a 3-finalist shortlist) is a travel eSIM service built vertical-first for cabin crew, starting with Emirates and expanding to Etihad → JAL → ANA. The wedge is a schedule-upload feature: crew screenshot their monthly Crew Pulse roster, our OCR pipeline reads the legs, and the app pre-arms the right local data plan at every layover. The Michelin-style crew-curated recommendations layer is the long-term moat — eSIM is just the wedge to acquire crew users.

Why crew first: they're high-frequency international travelers (8–12 layovers/month), they share rosters with each other (built-in viral loop), they congregate in WhatsApp groups, and their layover patterns are deterministic (we know exactly which countries and how many GB before they land). Emirates DXB-based crew = ~22,000 people; addressable seed market.

Revenue model: SaaS-style subscription with a cabin-class tier ladder (Economy / Roster Bundle / Premium / Business / First / Cockpit) where higher tiers buy data pools + carryover + perks. Wholesale is via a Tier-3 sub-reseller (currently Samurai WiFi, soon directly via eSIM Access or eSIM Go). Our gross margin engine is the gap between $1.50–4.00/GB wholesale and $5–9/GB retail, amplified by breakage (users buy a 10 GB pool, consume 6 GB → we pocket the 4 GB not consumed) and pool carryover progression.

4.3 Refund logic — 6 named scenarios

Akito + I locked these on 2026-05-24. Every refund request maps to one of the six:

  1. Activation failed — full refund, no questions
  2. Coverage gap (country not actually served despite map) — full refund
  3. Wrong cabin tier purchased — upgrade/downgrade, prorated
  4. Unused before expiry — credit, not cash (preserves breakage P&L)
  5. Pilot/crew schedule cancelled — credit toward next month
  6. Force majeure (illness, airline strike) — case-by-case, document required

5. OPEN debates (live tradeoffs & my thinking)

5.1 "Porter Plan" rename for Roster Bundle

Balraj: Akito proposed renaming Roster Bundle → "Porter Plan" — metaphor is the airport porter who handles your luggage end-to-end. "You upload, we handle every leg, you don't think about it."

Why I like it: "Roster Bundle" is internal jargon. "Porter Plan" reads as a service promise. It also bridges to non-crew users (anyone with a multi-stop trip).

Concern: Porter is a common English word, USPTO Class 38 may be crowded. Need to verify.

My current lean: Rename, but block the TM clearance first. If Porter is dirty, fall back to: "Pairing Plan" (crew lingo for a multi-leg trip) or "Roster Plan".

Status: Awaiting TM check

5.3 Pool sizing for Business tier

Balraj: I had Business at 8 GB. Akito: "How come 8 GB pool? Premium is already 10."

He was right — tier progression broke. I fixed to 15 GB. The math: Premium 10 → Business 15 → First 25 maintains ~1.5× steps without exploding into pure breakage territory.

Cockpit pool was also wrong: had 30 GB, actual is 12 GB monthly + 12 GB lifetime emergency bank. The emergency bank handles diverted flights / unexpected layovers without forcing pilots to top up mid-trip. Lifetime cap prevents abuse.

Status: Fixed in xlsx

5.4 Carryover percentages

I had 30 / 50 / 80% Premium / Business / First. Akito: "wrong."

Actual locked: 50% / 70% / 100% × 2 months. The 100% × 2 months for First creates real "deposit account" feel — users see the value of staying subscribed even in low-travel months.

P&L implication: higher carryover = lower breakage = thinner gross margin on premium tiers, offset by lower churn.

Status: Fixed in xlsx

5.5 Should we model PAYG (Economy) users?

Akito: "We have basic plan pay for 1 GB and then refuel when needed — no subscription. Are we considering those users as well?"

I had been modeling Economy too simplistically (1 GB/month, flat). That undersells the PAYG cohort. Revised model: 2.5 GB/month per active PAYG user + 20% refuel attach rate = ~$13.68 monthly revenue per PAYG user. PAYG is now a real revenue line, not a rounding error.

Why this matters strategically: PAYG is the on-ramp. A crew member who's skeptical of subscriptions tries Economy, sees it works, upgrades to Roster Bundle. PAYG conversion rate to subscription = a metric I should track.

Status: Fixed in xlsx

5.7 Direct Carrier Entitlement vs Universal Link for iOS

Apple Carrier Entitlement = the "real" way: your eSIM brand appears as a Carrier in iOS Settings, with full integration. Requires 4–6 months of MNO partnership negotiation and per-MNO certification.

Universal Link = the workaround: tap a link, iOS provisions the eSIM via standard flow.

My call: Universal Link for v1. Carrier Entitlement is a v2+ project once we have leverage with MNOs. Skipping it shaves 4–6 months off our launch timeline.

Status: Decided 2026-05-24

5.8 Closed gating vs open public launch

Crew SIM (our direct competitor) gates via pilot-founder vouching + crew ID verification. That's their moat AND their bottleneck.

My thinking: we go closed-by-default for v1 (crew WhatsApp invite-only, no public sign-ups) but public-positioned. Public site says "premium travel eSIM" — only crew get the deep features (roster upload, crew recs). When we expand to general travel users in phase 4, the closed crew layer becomes the loyalty/status layer, not the entire product.

This is the backdoor strategy in 4.4.

Status: Decided

P&L model — assumption highlights

  • Tier mix (researched): Economy 50%, Roster Bundle 18%, Premium 13%, Business 8%, First 5%, Cockpit 3%. Pulled from Airalo cohort data + Holafly + RevenueCat travel-app benchmarks.
  • Refuel attach rate: 30% of Economy users top up at least once per month.

13. The thinking process (rejected paths)

Why crew-vertical instead of "general travel eSIM"

I considered going broad like Airalo or Holafly — wide catalog, paid acquisition, scale on SEO. Killed because: CAC at general-travel scale is $8–25 per user, payback period 4–8 months, and we'd be competing on brand recognition where Airalo has 5+ year head start. Crew-vertical has $0 organic CAC for the first 5K via WhatsApp groups, and the deep-feature moat (roster upload + crew recs) is uncopiable by general players.

Why Emirates first instead of all Middle East crew

Emirates is the largest crew base in the world (~22,000 cabin crew + 4,000 pilots DXB-based). Etihad is ~6,000. Qatar is ~13,000 but DOH-based crew have different telecom regulations. Starting Emirates means our OCR pipeline only has to handle one roster format (Crew Pulse) and one base airport (DXB). Etihad expansion is phase 2 because their roster format is different and the v2 pipeline lift is incremental.

Why not freemium

Firsty's free-with-ads model exists and gets users — but breaks at high-data usage. Crew use 3–5 GB/month minimum. A free tier funded by ads would lose money on every crew user. Economy PAYG is our "near-free" entry point — $1 GB top-ups feel cheap, but every top-up is gross margin positive.

Why not flat-rate unlimited like Holafly

Tested in the model: at crew consumption levels, flat-rate at competitive price points would break our gross margin within 30 days. The cabin-class tier ladder captures the same "I want to know what I pay" simplicity for users while preserving breakage and tier upgrade revenue.

Why we ditched the Stitch v4 prototype

Two hours spent across 5 architectures. Each iframe/CSS fix uncovered new edge cases. Lesson burned in: Stitch outputs are visual mockups, not interaction-wired UI. Each generated HTML has its own Tailwind config and viewport-relative positioning. Wrapping them retroactively into a clickable prototype matching v3 quality is a CSS treadmill. Path forward when we revisit: hand-rebuild from scratch using Stitch as visual reference only, or use gallery view and stop trying to make it clickable.

Why a separate holding company at all

Apple Inc. owns iPhone. Moonlight Holdings will own [service brand]. This separates: (a) USPTO classes (35 for Moonlight vs 9/38 for service brand) — different filing strategies; (b) future M&A optionality — we can sell the service brand without unwinding the holding entity; (c) personal liability — Balraj and Akito are insulated by an extra corporate layer; (d) UAE tax exposure — Moonlight is the US tax entity, UAE operations sit underneath.

Why Vivek's residential address instead of a virtual mailbox

Mercury Bank flags virtual mailboxes during onboarding. Stripe Atlas works with them but slower. Apple Developer Program may reject them. UAE Permanent Establishment defense is stronger with a real US residence. Vivek lives in Texas — no state income tax — and is already a trusted partner from TQHB. Trade-off accepted: we pay him a small monthly stipend for mail handling.

Why I chose Sonnet for sub-agents instead of Opus

Researcher / Coder / Banana Coder / GitHub Coder / Akito's Assistant / Baru's Assistant / Lead Finder all run mechanical work (web scraping, code generation, image gen, summarization, repo commits). Sonnet 4.6 is 3–5× faster and ~5× cheaper for this work. Opus stays on the main thread where judgment and architectural decisions live. This split lets me run 1–2 background Researchers continuously while we talk.
