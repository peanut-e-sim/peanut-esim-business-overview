1. Why this tab matters

Every other tab in this model assumes Option A pricing ($2.26/GB blended after the 75/20/5 geo mix — corrected 2026-06-02, old $1.34/GB invalidated). The procurement decision is the single biggest COGS lever in the entire business. Moving from Option A to Option C at 5,000 users would save roughly $19,300/month based on corrected rates (rebuilt 2026-06-02 with corrected rate sheet).

The reason this tab exists is to make the procurement roadmap visible and intentional, not reactive. We don't want to renegotiate supplier terms in an emergency — we want to approach that conversation with volume data, real competitive quotes, and a clear alternative (Option C) as leverage.

Note on Option B math at single-user level

Option B's breakage tax (15% on unused inventory) means you pre-buy a pool assuming users will consume it. At 4 GB consumed from a 10 GB pool, 6 GB is "unused" — but you pre-bought it. The 15% breakage is modeled as an additional cost on the under-consumed portion. In practice, at scale the math averages out differently, but Balraj's worked example above shows the single-user case to make the concept clear.

3. The discussion we had

Discussion notes — 2026-06-01 bite-by-bite walkthrough

  • Why Option A is called "UNCERTAIN": We have access to the wholesale supplier's published rate sheet. We have not signed a contract or negotiated terms. The model assumes we can buy at list rate — this may or may not be available or may require volume minimums. Needs a direct conversation with the supplier.
  • Why Option B is "CONFIDENT": Balraj said he is confident a bulk prepay arrangement can be negotiated with the current wholesale supplier. This is the fallback if Option A pay-per-order isn't available or has unfavorable terms. The 15% breakage tax is an estimate of the cost of over-buying inventory.
  • Why Option C is "PENDING RESEARCH": The direct wholesale research file was produced 2026-06-01 identifying eSIM Access direct, eSIM Go, and Telnyx as potential upstream providers. The 40% saving is an estimate based on typical supplier margin analysis, not a real quote from any of these providers. Research was in progress at time of this document.
  • We considered going direct at launch: Rejected. The $5,000/month fixed platform fee is a major drag at low user counts. At 50 users (launch), the platform fee alone would cost $100/user/month — wiping out all margins. There is no reason to take on that cost before we have volume to dilute it.
  • We discussed whether Option B is ever the right answer: Option B (bulk prepay) is only better than A if the breakage tax on unused inventory is lower than the 10% saved on COGS. At high consumption rates (e.g., Power Crew archetype) it works. At low consumption rates (Casual Travelers) it doesn't. The answer depends on our actual user mix — which we don't have data on yet.
  • We discussed wholesaler negotiation strategy: Wholesalers do not drop prices voluntarily. The levers are: (1) volume — "we're at 2,000 users and growing," (2) competition — showing real quotes from direct providers, (3) direct access — credibly threatening to bypass them. Without all three, the conversation starts from weakness.
  • The supplier margin spread (40-130x): Research showed the wholesale markup over upstream direct rates is extremely wide — between 40x and 130x depending on market. This is the commercial opportunity in Option C. The research file is at direct-supplier-research-2026-06-01.html.
Rejected approach: Lock into a bulk prepay contract at launch.
Reason: No data yet on actual consumption patterns. Buying a large pool without knowing how much crew actually use risks paying a large breakage tax on phantom inventory. Wait for real usage data post-launch before committing to any prepay.
Rejected approach: Build direct provider relationship before launch.
Reason: Direct provider contracts (eSIM Access, eSIM Go, Telnyx) require business validation, API integration work, and volume minimums. Pre-launch we have none of these. The payoff only materialises above 1,724 users anyway — the work is better done closer to that milestone.

5. The crossover math

Option C's advantage per user on COGS: $2.26 − $1.36 = $0.90/GB (rebuilt 2026-06-02 with corrected rate sheet). For a typical Premium user consuming 4 GB, that's $3.60/user saved on data. The blended saving per user across all tiers is approximately $4.86/user/month (at ~5.4 GB blended consumption).

Option C's fixed platform fee: $5,000/month (directional estimate — not a real quote).

Crossover point: $5,000 / $4.86 = ~1,029 users (was 1,724 at old rates — crossover is now earlier because absolute COGS saving per user is larger).

7. Strategic takeaway

The procurement strategy has three phases:

  1. Launch on Option A (M1-M10, 0-1,500 users): Pay-per-order, no inventory risk, no upfront commitment. Negotiate terms with wholesale supplier early — even a small improvement from the list rate improves all downstream economics.
  2. Get research-backed quotes at 1,500 users (M9-M11): By this point we have real usage data, a proof-of-concept business, and a coherent story for direct providers. Get actual quotes from eSIM Access, eSIM Go, Telnyx. Validate or revise the −40% assumption.
  3. Negotiate the switch above 1,724 users (M12+): Approach wholesale supplier with direct-provider quotes in hand. Either they improve terms (wins Option A/B) or we switch to direct (wins Option C). Wholesalers don't negotiate without leverage — volume + alternatives is that leverage.

The most important insight from this tab: the wholesale markup is 40-130x over upstream rates. This means there is enormous room for cost improvement. We are currently leaving material margin on the table as the price of a fast, simple launch. That is a reasonable tradeoff — but the roadmap to capture that margin should be explicit and time-bound.
