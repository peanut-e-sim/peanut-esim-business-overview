Executive Summary

Top 3 Recommendations & Key Findings

Bottom line: The single highest-leverage action available to Peanut before launch is approaching eSIM Access (the current candidate's upstream Tier-2 supplier) directly. Their published claim of "up to 65% cheaper than competitors," confirmed no-MOQ self-serve onboarding, verified same-SIM top-up API, and Apple official recognition make them the strongest alternative by a significant margin. eSIM Go is the best true Tier-2 backup with fully published pricing tiers and a startup-accessible $1k minimum. Airalo Partner API rounds out the top three as a no-commitment, self-serve option with 200+ destination coverage and confirmed Apple Universal Link support.

The council's supplier concentration risk flag is well-founded: Peanut's entire unit economics model rests on a single undisclosed rate card from a Tier-3 reseller. The research confirms that going one or two tiers up the stack is operationally feasible within 2-4 weeks and could reduce blended wholesale cost by an estimated 25-40%, without any meaningful change to the technical integration (same API underneath).

Recommendation 1 — eSIM Access (Tier 2): GSMA-certified, Qualcomm-backed, Apple-recognized, confirmed same-SIM top-up, no minimum order quantity, self-serve onboarding. This is the current candidate's own upstream supplier. Approaching them directly is not technically difficult and the API is identical. The main risk is a potential exclusivity clause in the current candidate agreement — review contract before contact. Send the first email this week.

Recommendation 2 — eSIM Go (Tier 2, MVNO enabler): UK-based, fresh TNS Global investment (May 2026), 190+ countries on Tier-1-only networks. Published pricing tiers: Standard starts at $1k minimum top-up with $100 test credit — lowest barrier to entry of any serious T2 platform. MVNO-in-a-box capability is relevant for Peanut v2. Onboarding is self-serve. API includes real-time usage data, location callbacks, and installation status tracking. Rate deck is not public but can be obtained by signing up for a Standard account.

Recommendation 3 — Airalo Partner API (Tier 3, but tier-leader): World's largest eSIM marketplace with 20M+ users, 200+ destinations, Apple Universal Link confirmed, sandbox environment available, no subscription fee or minimum commitment. The downside: Airalo sits between you and the network, so margins are thinner than going T2 direct. But the risk profile is the lowest of any option — they have signed 6,500+ partners, including pre-revenue companies, with minimal friction. Use as insurance / dual-supply while the T2 relationship matures.

Surprise findings: (1) eSIM.tech (Netherlands) offers pay-per-MB with confirmed webhooks, same-SIM top-up, sandbox, and real-time usage — all critical Peanut features — at approximately €1.20/GB, potentially the lowest per-GB rate found. The PAYG model creates margin volatility for fixed-price crew plans, but the API parity is impressive for a small Dutch entity. (2) Telnyx is IoT-only at $12.80/GB baseline — completely unsuitable for travel eSIM; eliminated. (3) GigSky wholesale rates likely land at ~$3.14/GB even at 50% discount — above target. (4) UAE regulatory context: the TDRA travel eSIM restriction affects in-UAE purchases, not pre-installed roaming profiles. Crew who install before departure are not affected by UAE blocking rules.

UAE Regulatory Context (Applies to All Suppliers): TDRA technically restricts sale of travel eSIMs to users already inside the UAE to protect local MNOs (Etisalat/du). In practice, eSIM providers whose apps/sites are accessible outside UAE are unaffected. For Peanut: Emirates crew install their eSIM from home-country devices (Tokyo, London, Singapore) before travel. Once installed, the eSIM profile routes as international roaming via the supplier's home APN (typically Frankfurt/Amsterdam/London), bypassing UAE DPI entirely. VoIP calls (WhatsApp, FaceTime audio/video) remain blocked at the network level in UAE regardless of eSIM provider — this is a product communication issue, not a supplier-selection issue.
