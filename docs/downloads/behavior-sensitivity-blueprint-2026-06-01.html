1. Why this tab matters

Tabs 1-14 model a single steady-state. Tab 17 is the first tab to ask: "what if our assumptions about WHO our users are turn out to be wrong?" Specifically, what if most users stay on Economy forever? What if crew use more data than we assumed? What if the mix shifts toward high-value subscribers faster than expected?

The tab is a stress-test. It does not change the base case — it quantifies how much that base case could be wrong in either direction.

5. The discussion we had

Discussion — the key finding: tier mix dominates archetype mix

We set up the tab expecting both layers (archetype mix and tier mix) to have roughly equal impact on profit. The discovery was that tier mix is the dominant variable. Here is why:

  • Archetype differences affect how many GB are consumed from a fixed pool. But the gross margin percentage stays roughly stable (73% across most tiers) regardless of consumption rate. Consuming more GB increases COGS but also enables refuel revenue at the same margin. The net per-user profit doesn't change dramatically between a Casual Traveler and a Power Crew on the same tier.
  • Tier differences affect revenue directly and massively. The delta between an Economy user ($9.13 contribution) and a Business user ($21.86 contribution) is $12.73/user/month — 140% more per user for choosing a different plan. No archetype difference comes close to this magnitude.
  • Therefore: every dollar invested in converting Economy users to subscription tiers delivers roughly 5-10x the return vs investing in reducing churn of high-consumption users.

Discussion — how we set the tier scenario percentages

The percentages in each tier scenario are informed guesses (labeled "educated guesses" in the xlsx). They are not based on hard data for a crew-targeted eSIM product — no such comparable data exists. We designed them to represent plausible market positions:

  • Launch Reality (80% Economy): Based on the observed pattern in consumer subscription apps that most new signups start on the cheapest option. 80% is probably conservative — some crew will immediately recognize the subscription value — but it is honest.
  • Steady State (50% Economy): Our base case. We are betting that over time, approximately half of users will upgrade to a subscription tier. This is an informed bet, not a measured outcome.
  • PAYG Trap (70% Economy): What happens if we fail to execute on the conversion funnel. The business is still profitable but significantly less so. The trap is not fatal — it just leaves money on the table.
  • Ladder Climbers (35% Economy): What happens if onboarding is strong, the value of subscription is immediately obvious, and social proof accelerates upgrades. Achievable with excellent product design.
  • Mature Crew (30% Economy): Year 2+ expectation. By this point, early Economy users have either upgraded, churned, or settled into a pattern. A 30% Economy base is the floor for a well-run subscription business.

Discussion — why One-and-Done is specifically dangerous

The One-and-Done archetype has a 30% monthly churn rate. This is dangerous not just for unit economics but for the viral growth loop. If 30% of users disappear after one trip, they never become the people who tell their crewmates about Peanut. The entire word-of-mouth growth model depends on users having at least 2-3 monthly cycles before they form habits and referral behavior. One-and-Done users essentially never enter the referral funnel. At scale, a One-and-Done-heavy user base means the S-curve in Tab 16 never steepens — the business grows only as fast as Akito can recruit personally.

7. The conclusion (what we decided)

The discussion produced a clear, decisive conclusion:

  1. Conclusion 1 — Tier mix is the primary profit lever. The tab confirmed that converting Economy users to any subscription tier is more valuable than almost any other operational improvement. A 1% shift in the Economy percentage at 5,000 users = $50 × ($13.36 − $9.13) = ~$2,115/month permanently. This is not incremental — it compounds with user count.
  2. Conclusion 2 — The PAYG Trap is the primary risk. Not the One-and-Done archetype. If users are perfectly happy buying $4.99 top-ups forever and never feel the need to subscribe, we are in the PAYG Trap. The product design (the 3-PAYG purchases = Premium Economy upsell nudge) is specifically designed to prevent this.
  3. Conclusion 3 — Archetype mix should be monitored but not over-optimized. There is not much point spending marketing budget to attract Power Crew over Crew Standard — the per-user profit difference is small. Focus acquisition on crew (not casual travelers) to avoid One-and-Done, but within that constraint, any crew archetype is fine.

9. Strategic takeaway

Two action items came out of this tab:

  1. Build the conversion funnel into the product before launch. The moment a user makes their 3rd Economy purchase in a single month, the app should show: "You spent $14.97 this month. Premium Economy is $17.99 for 10 GB — and works everywhere without buying per-trip." This in-product prompt is the single most valuable feature for profitability. Akito should spec this as a v1.0 feature, not a v1.1 feature.
  2. Track tier distribution from Week 1. Balraj should set up a simple monthly report: what percentage of active users are on each tier? Compare against the Launch Reality baseline. If by M3 the Economy percentage is tracking higher than 80%, the conversion funnel is not working and needs adjustment before the numbers compound into the PAYG Trap.
