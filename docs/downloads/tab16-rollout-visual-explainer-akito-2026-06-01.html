7The dangerous churn assumption

5% monthly churn is the number the whole curve rests on. Industry disagrees.

5% monthly churn — our thesis vs the industry

Scenario Monthly churn M24 users M24 net/mo Verdict
Our thesis 5.0% 5,000 $52,650 Crew = recurring fliers, sticky by nature
Industry average 8-12% ~2,800 – 3,200 ~$29K – $34K One-trip purchasers, never return

Why we think 5% holds for crew:

  • Crew fly every month — data need is structural, not trip-dependent
  • CrewLink roster upload = monthly re-engagement hook built into the product
  • Crew recs + accommodation layers create non-eSIM reasons to open the app
  • Cabin-class brand identity = social currency inside the crew community
What breaks if we're wrong: If churn runs at 8% instead of 5%, the M24 user base falls from 5,000 to approximately 3,000 and monthly net contribution drops from $52,650 to ~$31,000. The estimated breakeven slides from M11-M12 to approximately M14-M15 (both figures assume $11K/mo fixed burn — PLACEHOLDER). This is the single biggest assumption to validate in the first 3 months post-launch.

9The Akito ask

Two things that will confirm or break the entire 24-month model. Both are field-validation tasks.

WHAT NEEDS TO BE TRUE FOR THIS CURVE TO HOLD
Two numbers to validate before we lock the model.

(1) Actual M1 cost per user acquired.
The model assumes $8 CAC — based on organic crew-network referrals. Before month 3, measure what you actually spend per new subscriber (time cost + any paid spend). If the real number is above $12, the CAC assumption needs updating and the cash curve widens.

(2) M3-M4 retention rate (proxy for monthly churn).
Look at the first crew who signed up in M1. What percentage are still active by M3-M4? If it is 85%+ (i.e., 5% monthly churn confirmed), the whole model stands. If it falls below 75% (i.e., 8%+ churn), the TRUE breakeven slides and the funding ask needs to increase. This one number is the most important field data point in the first year.
