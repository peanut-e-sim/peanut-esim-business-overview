1. What Tab 13 Is Supposed to Do

Tab 13 tracks the total cash going out of the business: fixed costs (registered agent, Apple account), variable costs (Stripe fees), one-time costs (LLC filing), and founder draws when applicable. It feeds into the P&L model as the OPEX line.

Its job is not to model costs — it is to record costs. Until a receipt exists, the number should be $0. The tab should look like a bank statement, not a projection spreadsheet.

2. The $11K Placeholder Problem

The original Tab 13 in the P&L model contains a $11,000/month OPEX assumption. This figure drove several downstream calculations including:

  • A paper "breakeven" at approximately Month 11-12 in Tab 16
  • A framing of "the business will lose money for the first 6-9 months"
  • An investor-grade runway calculation that implied Balraj needed $60-100K in startup capital
All of this is fiction.

None of the $11K has been spent. There is no developer on payroll. There is no marketing budget. There are no servers, no SaaS tools, no office. The $11K was a placeholder inserted to make the model look like a "real" startup for investor review purposes. For an operator-run business built on word-of-mouth, it is completely wrong.

The correct OPEX model:

Record actual expenses row by row as they are incurred. Year 1 total real OPEX is likely under $1,000 (LLC filing + Apple account + Vivek address × 12 months). The model's $11K/month figure should be replaced with real numbers before any investor-facing use.

Why the $11K figure even exists

The Tab 16 rollout model (which feeds from Tab 13) was built with an $1.80/user/month OPEX assumption — a scaled version of the $11K fixed cost spread over users. This number is also a placeholder. The correct OPEX at launch is $0/user/month fixed + Stripe variable fees only. Both the Tab 13 and Tab 16 OPEX assumptions should be treated as hypothetical until real costs are tracked.

4. The Monthly Close Routine

This should take 1 hour per month, maximum. The routine keeps Tab 13 accurate without becoming a burden:

  1. Export Mercury bank statement (PDF)
    Download the monthly statement. This is the source of truth — every business expense should flow through Mercury, never a personal card.
  2. Log each transaction in Wave
    Match every bank line to a Wave category. Wave imports from bank automatically — just confirm or correct the category. Takes 10 minutes with clean bank data.
  3. Update Tab 13 in the P&L spreadsheet
    Add any new recurring costs as rows. Update the total OPEX cell. The model recalculates automatically. Keep categories identical to Wave for easy cross-reference.
  4. Check Stripe payout reconciliation
    Stripe dashboard shows net payouts (after fees). Confirm the payout landed in Mercury. Stripe fees for the month = total gross revenue × 2.9% + (number of transactions × $0.30).
  5. Run a 5-minute P&L check
    Net profit = gross revenue - COGS - OPEX - Stripe fees. If this number is positive and growing, the business is healthy. The monthly close is done.

5. The Cockpit Cash Advantage

The Cockpit annual prepay plan ($299/yr) creates a structural cash float that reduces the need for startup capital. When a user pays $299 upfront:

Runway at $0 OPEX Infinite Infinite Infinite

Why this matters for burn planning

At $0 fixed OPEX, the Cockpit prepay does more than earn interest — it eliminates the concept of "runway." The business has no monthly cash outflow to sustain. Every Cockpit annual sold is an immediate positive cash event that funds all potential future fixed costs many times over. At 100 Cockpit users, the $14,950 float more than covers the entire estimated Year 1 OPEX of $2,500. This is why "push Cockpit from day one" is the single most leverage-efficient product decision in the model.

7. Why This Business May Never Need Outside Capital

Self-funded thesis
At $0 OPEX, every dollar of contribution is profit. There is no cash gap to bridge.

Most startups need capital because they spend before they earn — salaries, servers, and marketing eat cash for months before revenue arrives. Peanut has none of those costs at launch. The eSIM service is delivered via the wholesale supplier's infrastructure (no servers). Marketing is Akito's word-of-mouth (no budget). The founders take no salary (no payroll). The only costs that exist are incurred once and are trivially small. This makes Peanut self-funding from the first transaction.

What would require outside capital

  • Hiring a full-time developer before launch
  • Running paid ads before organic channels are maxed
  • Taking founder salaries before the business can support them
  • Building a customer service team before AI support is maxed

None of these are Year 1 decisions. All of them are avoidable by design.

What keeps burn at $0

  • No full-time hires — Akito and Balraj as founders
  • No paid marketing — crew WhatsApp network only
  • No custom infrastructure — wholesale eSIM provider handles delivery
  • No office — 100% remote, no Dubai co-working space

Each line item is a decision to maintain, not an accident. Protect them deliberately.
