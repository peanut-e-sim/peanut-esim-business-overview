# Privacy Policy Competitive Analysis — Travel eSIM Brands
**Prepared for:** Peanut eSIM — Phase 1, Item 4 (Terms / Privacy / Refund Policy)  
**Date:** 2026-05-24  
**Scope:** 6 competitors; cross-referenced against PDPL (UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45/2021), GDPR (EU 2016/679), CCPA (California)

---

## 1. COMPETITOR POLICIES AT A GLANCE

### 1.1 Airalo
**Source URL:** https://www.airalo.com/more-info/privacy-policy  
**Last updated:** March 2026  
**Entity:** AirGSM Pte Ltd, Singapore  
**Estimated word count:** ~3,500

**Data collected:** Name, email, postal address, phone, IP, device/browser, location (IP-based + GPS), cookie identifiers, payment data, biometrics (eKYC countries), social media login tokens, survey/review data, user-generated content.

**Location data:** Explicitly calls out "specific location data derived from your mobile device" and "non-specific location based on your IP address." No special handling clause for roster uploads.

**Retention:** "Only as long as we need it, or are required legally to retain it." No specific periods stated except eKYC obligation carve-out.

**Third-party processors named:** Trustpilot (reviews). Stripe implied (footer branding "StripeM-Inner"). No exhaustive sub-processor list published.

**Children:** Age 13 cutoff. Contact: legal@airalo.com.

**International transfers:** SCCs for EEA transfers. Country-specific addenda: Saudi Arabia (SDAIA), Colombia, Malaysia (PDPA), Philippines, South Africa (POPIA), US (CCPA).

**User rights:** Access, correction, erasure, restriction, portability, objection, marketing opt-out. Rights exercisable in-app or via legal@airalo.com.

**Cookie section:** References separate Cookie Policy. "Manage cookies" footer link present.

**Notable strength:** The "Compliance with local regulations" section is genuinely impressive — it has a KSA-specific PDPL addendum mentioning SDAIA as the regulator. This is the only competitor that names the Saudi regulator explicitly.

**Notable weakness:** Saudi PDPL addendum but ZERO mention of UAE PDPL (Federal Decree-Law No. 45/2021), even though UAE is a primary market. No sub-processor list. Retention periods are vague.

### 1.2 Holafly
**Source URL:** https://esim.holafly.com/privacy-policy/  
**Entity:** Holafly Limited, 6th Floor, 2 Grand Canal Square, Dublin 2, Ireland  
**Estimated word count:** ~2,800

**Data collected:** Full name, title, country of residence, current location, residential address, network provider, phone number, payment info, email, device type, passport/ID copy (where necessary), IP address, location data, device metadata.

**Location data:** "current location" collected as part of Customer Data. No explicit roster/occupational data category. Network carrier sees IMSI, MSISDN, IP only.

**Retention:** Explicit! "Length of contractual relationship plus **7.5 years** after termination." Call recordings: **6 months**. Connection history: **6 months**. This is the most specific retention schedule of any competitor.

**Third-party processors named:** No named list of processors — only categories (administrative, advertising, IT services, telecom partners). DPO contact: dpo@group.holafly.com.

**Children:** Age 18 cutoff (stricter than most — uses Irish law standard). Active deletion + account termination policy.

**International transfers:** SCCs (Module 2). Names Thailand and Colombia as transfer destinations. DPO email for transfer details.

**User rights:** Full GDPR suite in table format (Access, Object, Rectification, Erasure, Restriction, Portability, Complaint, Automated-decision objection). Supervisory authority: Irish DPC (www.dataprotection.ie).

**Cookie section:** Separate Cookies Policy linked. Consent required for non-essential cookies.

**Notable strength:** Best-in-class for structure and GDPR compliance. The retention table with actual years (7.5y / 6mo) is exactly what regulators want. The DPO is named and reachable.

**Notable weakness:** Zero mention of UAE PDPL. No CCPA section. Processor list is category-only (no named vendors).

### 1.3 Saily
**Source URL:** https://saily.com/legal/privacy-policy/  
**Entity:** Nord Security group (NordVPN parent)  
**Last updated:** 2025-11-07  
**Estimated word count:** ~5,000+ (most comprehensive of all 6)

**Data collected:** Account info, purchase/payment history, device info, IP, location (auto top-up), usage data. B2B section covers employee/contractor data.

**Location data:** Mentions "approximate location based on IP address" for auto top-up. Separate B2B section for employee data.

**Retention:** States personal data is destroyed using methods preventing recovery. References legal obligation retention. No specific periods stated, but strong destruction language.

**Third-party processors named (explicitly listed):**
- Payment: Stripe Inc., Stripe Payments Europe Ltd., Adyen N.V., PayPal Inc., NordSec B.V., Moonflash Limited, Lagosec Inc., nordvpn S.A.
- Analytics: Google Analytics, Firebase Analytics (Google), AppsFlyer (AppsFlyer Ltd.)
- Attribution: Hasoffers (Tune Inc.)

**Children:** Age 18. Active deletion policy. No data from under-18s.

**International transfers:** Acknowledges cross-border transfers; users consent upon registration.

**User rights:** DARO rights: Delete, Access, Rectify, Object, Restrict, Portability. Exercisable via account or email.

**Cookie section:** Separate Cookie Policy. Consent-based for non-essential.

**Notable strength:** The sub-processor named list is the most transparent of all competitors. Having Stripe, Adyen, Google Analytics, Firebase, and AppsFlyer named by legal entity is exactly what Article 28 GDPR and PDPL Article 11 require for sub-processing transparency.

**Notable weakness:** No PDPL or CCPA section. Heavy NordVPN ecosystem language — can feel like the privacy policy wasn't truly written for an eSIM product.

### 1.4 Nomad (nomadesim.com)
**Source URL:** https://www.nomadesim.com/documents/privacy  
**Last updated:** March 2026  
**Entity:** LotusFlare (parent company)  
**Estimated word count:** ~4,000

**Data collected:** Name, email, payment info, device info, IP, location, log/usage data, social media login tokens, Firebase event data. Parent company LotusFlare monitors payment processor compliance.

**Location data:** "country, location" collected for service operation. No special handling for occupational data.

**Retention:** Deletion or anonymization when no longer needed. Files retained for fraud/legal compliance. Specific research project data deletion per-project.

**Third-party processors named:**
- Payment: PayPal, Stripe (linked to full privacy policies)
- Analytics: Firebase (Google)
- No exhaustive list

**Children:** Age 13 cutoff. GDPR age of consent awareness ("applicable age limit" language). Active deletion on discovery.

**International transfers:** Specifically names: Singapore, Australia, UK, US, Canada, China, Saudi Arabia as jurisdictions. Uses SCCs. This is unusually transparent geographic disclosure.

**User rights:** GDPR + CCPA both explicitly addressed. CCPA section names: Right to Know, Delete, Non-Discrimination, Opt-Out of Sale/Sharing. States "Nomad does not sell data."

**Cookie section:** Cookie consent management. Standard browser opt-out guidance.

**Notable strength:** Best CCPA implementation of the group. The explicit "we do not sell data" statement + opt-out mechanism is clean and defensible. Nomad Mar 2026 update is recent.

**Notable weakness:** No PDPL (UAE) section. "LotusFlare" as parent adds legal complexity that isn't well explained to users. No DPO named.

### 1.5 Crew SIM (crewsim.com)
**Source URL:** https://www.crewsim.com/en/privacy/  
**Entity:** Crewsim GmbH, Vienna, Austria  
**Effective date:** July 3, 2025  
**Estimated word count:** ~1,200 (shortest by far)

**Data collected:** Name, IP, company name, job title, address, email, phone, billing/credit card. Device info, browser type, operating system, URLs, crash data, cookie identifiers, log files, usage patterns, collaboration patterns.

**Location data:** "IP address and/or country preference in order to approximate your location." No roster/schedule data category. No mention of occupational data despite being crew-focused.

**Retention:** Not stated. Zero retention language. This is a major GDPR violation gap.

**Third-party processors named:** "advertising and analytics partners" — no named vendors. References a separate "Cookies and Tracking policy" but no link provided.

**Children:** Not mentioned. Complete omission — GDPR Article 8 violation gap.

**International transfers:** Not mentioned. Complete omission.

**User rights:** Only mentions: withdraw consent at any time. Full GDPR rights (Article 15-21) are not listed.

**Cookie section:** References "Cookies and Tracking policy" but no link. Cookie consent not described.

**Notable strength:** Simple, short, readable. The "we do not sell any data" statement is present and clear.

**Notable weakness:** This is the worst privacy policy of the group. Missing: retention periods, children section, international transfer language, full user rights, DPO/contact for privacy requests, named processors. For an Austrian GmbH, GDPR non-compliance gaps are significant. Also ironic: a crew-specific eSIM doesn't acknowledge occupational/roster data as a special category. Peanut handles exactly this data — Crew SIM's gap is your differentiator.

### 1.6 Yesim (yesim.app)

**Estimated word count:** ~3,500

**Data collected:** Identity info, payment method, date of birth, tax residence, address, email, phone, ID document (where legally required), call/data usage metadata, virtual number service data, device metadata, cookie/tracking identifiers, IP, browser info, form inputs, behavioral analytics.

**Location data:** "geolocation data, including information about the location of your purchase" — explicitly named in Service Usage Data section. Also captured via IP.

**Third-party processors named:** Categories only: cloud hosting, customer support platforms, analytics, payment/billing, email delivery, fraud detection. No named vendors. Also mentions connectivity/roaming partners, commercial partners, local service partners, professional advisors, group companies.

**Children:** Not explicitly mentioned. Gap.

**Notable weakness:** Swiss law focus (nFADP + GDPR) — no PDPL, no CCPA. No named sub-processors. No children's section. No specific retention periods.

## 3. VERDICT

**Best policy: Holafly**  
Reason: Actual retention periods (7.5 years / 6 months), a named DPO, a formal table of GDPR rights, and the most readable structure. An Irish-incorporated entity under the DPC gives it teeth. Quote worth stealing:

> *"Our network carriers only process the minimal technical information needed for the service to function, such as the IMSI (International Mobile Subscriber Identity), the MSISDN (mobile number identifier), and the IP addresses used to establish the connection... Our network partners only access technical data required for connectivity and cannot see details such as names, emails, payment information, or browsing activity."*  
> — Holafly Privacy Policy, Section 6 (https://esim.holafly.com/privacy-policy/)

This technical transparency about what network partners CAN and CANNOT see is a trust signal Peanut should replicate.

**Worst policy: Crew SIM**  
Reason: 1,200 words for an Austrian GmbH legally required to be GDPR compliant. Missing retention statement, children's clause, international transfer section, and full user rights list. No DPO, no supervisor authority named. A direct competitor with known regulatory exposure Peanut can exploit by being explicitly better.

## 5. PDPL-SPECIFIC CLAUSES COMPETITORS MISS (Peanut Differentiators)

The following are required or strongly advisable under UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45/2021 and its Executive Regulations. **No competitor addresses any of these:**

1. **Article 4 (Lawfulness of Processing):** State the specific legal basis for each processing activity under UAE law — not just GDPR lawful bases. UAE recognizes: consent, contractual necessity, legal obligation, vital interest, public task, legitimate interest.

2. **Article 11 (Disclosure to Third Parties):** Any transfer of UAE resident data to a third party (including Stripe, Firebase) requires a written agreement ensuring adequate protection. Peanut should state: *"We enter into data processing agreements with all sub-processors handling UAE resident data in compliance with Article 11 of UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45/2021."*

3. **Article 13 (Cross-Border Transfer):** Transfer of UAE data outside the UAE requires either (a) adequacy determination by the UAE Data Office, or (b) contractual safeguards. Peanut should state the UAE Data Office has been noted and SCCs or equivalent are in place.

4. **Article 16 (Data Subject Rights):** UAE residents have: right to access, correction, erasure, restriction, portability, objection to automated processing, withdrawal of consent. Peanut must explicitly list these as UAE rights (distinct from GDPR rights, even if parallel).

5. **Article 21 (Breach Notification):** UAE law requires notification to the UAE Data Office within 72 hours of a data breach affecting UAE residents. State this commitment explicitly — no competitor does.

6. **Occupational data sensitivity:** Roster screenshots contain airline name, employee ID patterns, layover cities, and scheduling patterns. While not a "special category" under GDPR, under UAE PDPL Article 4 the processing requires explicit legitimate interest justification. Peanut should treat it as sensitive and say so.

## 6. SENTENCE TO STEAL (adapted from Holafly)

Adapted for Peanut's crew context:

> *"Your roster and schedule data is used solely to recommend the right eSIM plan for your upcoming destinations. It is never shared with your airline, your employer, or any third party, and is deleted from our systems within 30 days of your last active session. Our network partners see only the technical data necessary to connect you — they cannot see your name, email, schedule, or payment details."*
