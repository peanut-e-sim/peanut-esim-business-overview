TLDR

The per-tier geo mix analysis replaces the single $1.34/GB flat cost with tier-specific blended costs that reflect WHERE each tier of crew actually roams. Economy users vacation in Las Vegas at $8.50/GB. Roster Bundle crew fly Mumbai layovers at $0.85/GB. The result: Economy's gross margin collapses from 89% (default) to 18.3% (reality). Roster Bundle holds strong at 65.7%. The business is sound — but the mix of who buys which plan matters enormously. Push crew (Roster Bundle users) into subscriptions. Don't chase mass-market vacationers at Economy rates.

Why This Tab Exists

The model was originally built with a single weighted blended cost of $1.34/GB based on the global Emirates route distribution (75% Tier-A, 20% Tier-B, 5% Tier-C). That assumption holds for the overall customer base, but it masks massive variance at the tier level.

The insight that drove this tab: different plan tiers self-select into different user types, and different user types fly different routes. A crew member buying the Roster Bundle is optimizing for their monthly roster — they know they're flying to Mumbai and Bangkok. An Economy PAYG buyer might be a crew member on a personal holiday to the USA, or someone who bought at the airport. These two users have completely different geo profiles, and therefore completely different underlying costs.

Discussion — What This Means

The core insight

The geo mix is not random — it's structurally linked to plan type. Roster Bundle users are professional crew buying specifically for their work routes. They know they're flying to Bangkok, Mumbai, Nairobi — all Tier-A. Economy users are often crew on personal holidays (Bali, NYC, Vegas) or non-crew who stumbled across the app — these are predominantly Tier-B and Tier-C destinations.

This creates a beautiful natural hedge for the business: the plan that attracts the most vacation usage (Economy PAYG) is also the one with the lowest upside — because vacation destinations are expensive roaming countries. Meanwhile, the plans designed for professional crew (Roster Bundle, Cockpit) have the best cost profile because crew layovers concentrate in Tier-A countries.

The implication: converting Economy users to Roster Bundle subscriptions is a double win — it increases ARPU and it shifts their geo mix toward cheaper corridors as they start using Peanut for work trips instead of personal vacations.

Why every tier shows a negative "vs Default"

The default flat $1.34/GB blended assumption was calibrated on the overall Emirates route distribution (75/20/5). But no individual tier hits that exact mix — Economy is far heavier on Tier-C, while Roster Bundle barely touches Tier-C. The result: the flat assumption is an average that understates costs for some tiers and overstates for others. Per-tier analysis always produces a "worse" picture vs the optimistic average, because the tiers with the most Tier-C usage (expensive) are also the lowest-revenue tiers.

Which scenario are we actually in?

At launch, with a crew-heavy user base recruited through Akito's Emirates network, Optimistic is plausible. As we scale and attract more casual traveler sign-ups (App Store organic, etc.), the mix shifts toward Realistic. The Pessimistic scenario is a stress test — useful for understanding the floor.

The practical implication: our go-to-market strategy (targeting crew, not mass market) is also our geo mix defense strategy. Every crew customer we acquire is a Tier-A heavy user. Every generic traveler we acquire dilutes toward Tier-C.

Emirates Route Research Context

The geo mix assumptions are grounded in publicly available Emirates route data:

  • 140 destinations across 85 countries total in the Emirates network
  • 67 layover countries (turnarounds excluded) — only these count for Peanut data usage
  • North America (Tier-C): 3 countries — USA, Canada, Mexico. All long-haul routes with 24–48h layovers. These are the expensive ones.
  • Africa (Tier-A): 22 countries — mostly 24h crew layovers in Nairobi, Johannesburg, Lagos, Accra
  • SE Asia (Tier-A): 8+ countries — Bangkok, Colombo, Manila, Singapore, Jakarta, Kuala Lumpur, Hong Kong, Manila
  • Indian Subcontinent (Tier-A): 5+ cities — Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore, Chennai, Hyderabad

What "layover" means vs "turnaround"

Emirates DXB-based crew have very short turnarounds (1–3 hours) on some routes — crew stay on the aircraft or in the terminal and do not activate roaming. A real layover is 18+ hours: crew go to a crew hotel, rest, and use the city. Only layovers count for this analysis. Short turnarounds — even if the aircraft lands in a Tier-C country — generate zero data usage.

The implication for the geo mix model: even though Emirates flies to 3 North American countries (Tier-C), not all of those flights generate crew data usage. Routes like DXB-ORD-DXB might have a 24h Chicago layover (Tier-C), but the DXB-MIA turn might only be 90 minutes. Knowing which routes are true layovers vs turnarounds is essential for calibrating the Tier-C exposure.

Strategic Takeaway

The strategy is already correct — now prove it with data. The crew-first go-to-market isn't just a distribution strategy; it's a unit economics strategy. Crew users self-select into Tier-A heavy geo mixes. Every crew user we acquire is a cheaper user to serve. Every non-crew vacation traveler we acquire is a more expensive user to serve at Economy PAYG rates. The nudge system (Economy-to-Roster-Bundle conversion after 3 refuels) is not just ARPU optimization — it's also geo mix optimization. After conversion, Yuki starts using Peanut for work trips (Tier-A), not just personal vacations (Tier-C).
