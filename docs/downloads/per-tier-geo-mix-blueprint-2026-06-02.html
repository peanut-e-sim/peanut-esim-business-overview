TLDR

The per-tier geo mix analysis replaces the single $2.26/GB flat blended cost with tier-specific effective costs that reflect WHERE each tier of crew actually roams. Economy users vacation in Las Vegas at $2.83/GB (Tier-C). Roster Bundle crew fly Mumbai layovers at $2.29/GB (Tier-A). The result: Economy's gross margin is 51.6% with geo vs 54.8% flat — tighter but not catastrophic. Roster Bundle falls to 43.9% (vs 44% flat — similar). The business is sound — but Cockpit at 34.4% is the thinnest tier and warrants close monitoring. Push Economy users toward Roster Bundle or subscription. Don't chase mass-market vacationers at Economy PAYG. (Rebuilt 2026-06-02 with corrected Tier-A/B/C rate sheet.)

Why This Tab Exists

The model was originally built with a now-invalidated flat cost of $1.34/GB (stale rate, per-country source not yet confirmed). That assumption was corrected on 2026-06-02 — real blended cost is $2.26/GB. The per-tier geo mix analysis now uses the corrected Tier-A ($2.29/GB), Tier-B ($1.99/GB), and Tier-C ($2.83/GB) rates throughout. The fundamental insight — that tier-specific geo mixes matter — remains valid.

The insight that drove this tab: different plan tiers self-select into different user types, and different user types fly different routes. A crew member buying the Roster Bundle is optimizing for their monthly roster — they know they're flying to Mumbai and Bangkok. An Economy PAYG buyer might be a crew member on a personal holiday to the USA, or someone who bought at the airport. These two users have completely different geo profiles, and therefore completely different underlying costs.

Discussion — What This Means

The core insight

The geo mix is not random — it's structurally linked to plan type. Roster Bundle users are professional crew buying specifically for their work routes. They know they're flying to Bangkok, Mumbai, Nairobi — all Tier-A. Economy users are often crew on personal holidays (Bali, NYC, Vegas) or non-crew who stumbled across the app — these are predominantly Tier-B and Tier-C destinations.

This creates a beautiful natural hedge for the business: the plan that attracts the most vacation usage (Economy PAYG) is also the one with the lowest upside — because vacation destinations are expensive roaming countries. Meanwhile, the plans designed for professional crew (Roster Bundle, Cockpit) have the best cost profile because crew layovers concentrate in Tier-A countries.

The implication: converting Economy users to Roster Bundle subscriptions is a double win — it increases ARPU and it shifts their geo mix toward cheaper corridors as they start using Peanut for work trips instead of personal vacations.

Why most tiers show negative "vs Default"

The default flat $2.26/GB blended assumption is calibrated on the overall Emirates route distribution (75/20/5). Most individual tiers diverge from that mix — Economy is heavier on Tier-C, Roster Bundle concentrates in Tier-A. With the corrected rate sheet, the Tier-C premium dropped dramatically (from $8.50 → $2.83), so tier-level deviations are now smaller in absolute dollar terms. Roster Bundle actually beats the flat assumption slightly (+$0.09) because its Tier-A heavy mix combines with the new rate compression. Only Cockpit shows meaningful underperformance vs flat due to its mid-tier mix weighting.

Which scenario are we actually in?

At launch, with a crew-heavy user base recruited through Akito's Emirates network, Optimistic ($2.26/GB) is plausible. As we scale and attract more casual traveler sign-ups (App Store organic, etc.), the mix shifts toward Realistic ($2.28/GB). The Pessimistic scenario ($2.37/GB) is a stress test — but notably, with corrected rates, the scenario spread is only $0.11/GB (vs a $2.50 gap in the old model). This is good news: geo mix uncertainty matters less than we previously feared.

The practical implication: our go-to-market strategy (targeting crew, not mass market) is still our geo mix defense strategy. Every crew customer we acquire is a Tier-A heavy user. Every generic traveler we acquire dilutes toward Tier-C. But the cost of that dilution is now modest, not catastrophic.

Emirates Route Research Context

The geo mix assumptions are grounded in publicly available Emirates route data:

  • 140 destinations across 85 countries total in the Emirates network
  • 67 layover countries (turnarounds excluded) — only these count for Peanut data usage
  • North America (Tier-C): 3 countries — USA, Canada, Mexico. All long-haul routes with 24–48h layovers. These are the expensive ones.
  • Africa (Tier-A): 22 countries — mostly 24h crew layovers in Nairobi, Johannesburg, Lagos, Accra
  • SE Asia (Tier-A): 8+ countries — Bangkok, Colombo, Manila, Singapore, Jakarta, Kuala Lumpur, Hong Kong, Manila
  • Indian Subcontinent (Tier-A): 5+ cities — Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore, Chennai, Hyderabad

What "layover" means vs "turnaround"

Emirates DXB-based crew have very short turnarounds (1–3 hours) on some routes — crew stay on the aircraft or in the terminal and do not activate roaming. A real layover is 18+ hours: crew go to a crew hotel, rest, and use the city. Only layovers count for this analysis. Short turnarounds — even if the aircraft lands in a Tier-C country — generate zero data usage.

The implication for the geo mix model: even though Emirates flies to 3 North American countries (Tier-C), not all of those flights generate crew data usage. Routes like DXB-ORD-DXB might have a 24h Chicago layover (Tier-C), but the DXB-MIA turn might only be 90 minutes. Knowing which routes are true layovers vs turnarounds is essential for calibrating the Tier-C exposure.

Strategic Takeaway

The strategy is correct — and geo mix risk is smaller than feared. The crew-first go-to-market isn't just a distribution strategy; it's a unit economics strategy. Crew users self-select into Tier-A heavy geo mixes. With the corrected rate sheet (Tier-C now $2.83 not $8.50), the cost variance between crew and vacation users narrowed significantly — the business is profitable across all tiers even in Pessimistic mix. The nudge system (Economy-to-Roster-Bundle conversion after 3 refuels) is primarily ARPU optimization now, not survival-level cost management. Focus on Cockpit's 34.4% margin — that is the thinnest tier under the corrected model. (Rebuilt 2026-06-02)
