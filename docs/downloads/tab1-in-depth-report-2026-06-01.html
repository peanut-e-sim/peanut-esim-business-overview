3. Section 2 — Cabin Class Retail Prices (the locked ladder)

This is the single most important section in the entire workbook. Every revenue calculation downstream pivots from these 7 rows. Below are the locked prices, the reasoning, and the deep-dive on each tier (each plan has its own dedicated "Logic" tab in the workbook).

2.1 Economy (PAYG)

FieldValue

The 5-layer pricing logic behind $4.99

LayerRationale
1. Cost floorWholesale supplier's actual rate sheet for 1-GB short-trip plans: $0.56–1.25/GB in cheap zones (HK/Korea/Japan/Singapore/USA/UK). At $4.99 retail = 75-89% gross margin on typical crew trips.
2. Market middleAiralo 1 GB / 7-day USA ~$4.50, Roamless ~$4.00, Crew SIM $3-7 depending on country. We sit in the fair-middle, not the discount race.
3. PsychologicalUnder-$5 = impulse purchase zone. $5.99 = considered purchase. The 9-cents rule matters here.
4. Upsell anchor3 top-ups in a month = $14.97 → that's more than Premium Economy ($17.99 for 10 GB). The user does the math, upgrades.
5. Crew fit1 GB matches 1 layover. Selling 10 GB to a 1-trip crew traveler feels wasteful → churn risk.

Flat vs Zonal decision

Locked: Flat $4.99 worldwide for v1. Zonal pricing for India/Brazil/Africa in v1.1 (3-6 months post-launch).

Why flat: "Pay $5 anywhere you fly" is a clean marketing line. The ~10% of crew trips that happen in loss-making zones (India/Brazil/South Africa) are subsidized by the 90% in cheap zones (Asia/Europe/USA). At 10K Economy users, the worst-case loss is ~$2K/mo, dwarfed by $38K/mo profit from healthy zones. Net: +$36K/mo, defensible.

Backdoor closure

Economy is blocked for active subscribers in the app. Without this, a Business user paying $29.99/mo for 15 GB could buy 5 Economy top-ups at $4.99/GB instead of refueling at the old $5.99/GB — and save money. We close this by hiding Economy from subscribers entirely.

The fundamental insight that drove the design

A PAYG user with 5 scheduled flights in a month doesn't buy 5 SIMs. They buy 1-2 when they remember. Real PAYG behavior captures ~$8-11/mo per user. Roster Bundle's job is to capture the WHOLE month upfront — get the user to pre-commit to all 5 flights at a discount, lifting per-user revenue to $19.95 (5 × $3.99) with predictable cash flow.

Why the price was bumped from $12.99 to $17.99

The original $12.99 placeholder was cheaper than Roster Bundle ($19.95 for 5 flights). A crew member looking at the price card would see "Premium Economy is cheaper AND works anywhere" and choose it 100% of the time — cannibalizing Roster Bundle, which is the wedge feature.

$17.99 sits $2 below Roster Bundle, signaling "cheaper but you trade away auto-arming" — clean positioning, no cannibalization.

The 1-GB refill UX mechanic

Users see only 1 GB active at a time. When that GB runs out, they tap "Refill from pool" to get the next GB. The pool is always visible (transparency), but refill requires manual action.

Why this matters: friction-limited consumption drops from 62.5% (auto-deduct baseline) to ~40%. The unused 60% either rolls into carry or breaks. Pure breakage profit comes from forgetfulness, not deception.

Transparency policy (locked)

RuleReason
Pool is ALWAYS visibleNever hide what user paid for
Refill button is visible at all timesOne tap, no friction beyond explicit action
Day-25 reminder push: "You have X GB unused — refill?"Give them every chance to consume what they paid for
Carryover shown explicitly: "Y GB will carry, Z GB will expire"Honest disclosure
Breakage we earn from honest forgetfulness is fair revenue. Breakage we earn from hidden mechanics is fraud.

Why we created TWO First tiers (First + First Suite)

Original plan was a single First at $49.99 / 25 GB. But the gap from Business ($29.99 / 15 GB) to First ($49.99 / 25 GB) felt big and one-dimensional. Splitting into First $39.99/25GB and First Suite $49.99/40GB creates a clean ladder mirroring Emirates' actual cabin naming (First Class + First Class Suite). First captures purser-grade crew who feel above Business but not in "Suite" territory.

The 1-GB friction insight (locked-in understanding)

Pool expansion is essentially free to us. The 1-GB refill friction caps actual consumption at ~13-14 GB regardless of whether the pool is 15, 20, 25, or 40 GB. Bigger pool = better value perception, same COGS. This is why First's pool was bumped 20 → 25 GB without changing price.

Why refuel price was dropped from $5.99 to $4.99

The original $5.99 refuel rate was a "subscriber tax" — subscribers paid more per GB than PAYG users for the same product. That was both unfair and created the Economy backdoor. By matching refuel to Economy ($4.99) AND blocking Economy for subscribers, we close the loophole transparently.

Update 2026-06-01 — mix recalibrated from 60/25/15 to 75/20/5

The original 60/25/15 placeholder was set before we had a clear view of Emirates-specific route concentration. Emirates crew overwhelmingly fly Asia + Europe + USA (Tier-A), with relatively few rotations to Tier-C (India/Brazil/South Africa). Reweighting to 75% Tier-A / 20% Tier-B / 5% Tier-C drops the blended wholesale cost from $2.13 → $1.34/GB — a 37% reduction in unit COGS that flows through every per-tier P&L line.

Key insight from the wholesale supplier data audit

Sampling the supplier's actual settlement prices for 1-GB short-trip plans revealed that Tier-C destinations are WORSE than we estimated (India: $5.21 for 2-day, South Africa: $10.00 for 2-day). This makes the mix re-weighting even more material — at 5% Tier-C the expensive bucket barely moves the blended number; at 15% it dominated.

The real lever is the % mix, not the bucket prices. Emirates crew don't fly evenly across all 67 layover countries — they concentrate on Asia + Europe + USA. The 75/20/5 mix is the Emirates-specific reality.

Why the mix is flagged, not locked

The Emirates_Crew_Layover_Countries.xlsx file lists 67 countries (Africa 22, Europe 23, Asia Pacific 16, Americas 6) — but it gives country COUNT, not FLIGHT FREQUENCY. Real Emirates flight share is heavily Asia/Europe/USA, but we don't have the route-level data to validate exact percentages.

Validation path: Akito to collect 5-10 sample crew rosters → derive actual flight distribution → update the mix with real data.
